What is a Center of Excellence (COE)?
Organizational model · Strategic purpose · People, Process, Technology
COE

Core Definition

A Center of Excellence (COE) is a team within an organization that provides leadership, best practices, research, support, and training for Power Platform adoption. It balances innovation with control, scales citizen development, and connects low-code initiatives to measurable business outcomes.

What a COE IS

  • People: Admins, architects, champions, business liaisons
  • Process: Governance workflows, compliance gates, maker onboarding
  • Technology: COE Starter Kit, admin center, monitoring tools
  • A strategic capability, not just a toolset
  • Cultural change agent for the organisation

What a COE is NOT

  • NOT just the COE Starter Kit (tools are a means, not the end)
  • NOT a bottleneck or approval committee
  • NOT replacing PPAC — it complements it
  • NOT one-size-fits-all; every org customises theirs
  • NOT the sole owner of Power Platform resources

Three COE Pillars

Admin / Visibility

  • Full tenant inventory (apps, flows, makers)
  • Power BI adoption dashboards
  • Orphaned resource detection
  • Capacity monitoring & alerts
  • DLP impact analysis

Govern / Compliance

  • DLP policy design & enforcement
  • App compliance & audit workflows
  • Inactivity / quarantine processes
  • Environment request management
  • Teams environment governance

Nurture / Enablement

  • Maker onboarding & welcome emails
  • Training, hackathons, communities
  • Templates & reusable components
  • Internal app catalog
  • ROI measurement & showcases

Interview Q&A — Chapter 1

Q: How do you explain a COE to a non-technical stakeholder? ▼
"A COE is the team that makes sure Power Platform is used safely, effectively, and at scale across the organisation. Think of it like a control tower — we give makers the runway to build quickly while ensuring nothing dangerous takes off without a check. We provide guardrails, training, and visibility so the platform delivers real business value without creating technical debt or compliance risk."
Q: What is the difference between a COE and the COE Starter Kit? ▼
The COE is the organizational strategy — people, processes, governance model. The COE Starter Kit is a free, open-source reference implementation built in Power Platform that gives you a technical starting point. You still need to customise the kit to your org's needs. Note: Microsoft has announced the Starter Kit is no longer actively maintained; its core capabilities are migrating to Power Platform Admin Center.
Q: What are the three pillars of the COE Starter Kit? ▼
Admin (Core Components) — Syncs tenant inventory into Dataverse; Power BI dashboard; DLP impact analysis; permission management.

Govern (Governance Components) — Compliance processes, app quarantine, inactivity notifications, orphaned resource cleanup, Teams governance.

Nurture (Nurture Components) — Maker welcome emails, training materials, app catalog, community support, ROI tracking.
Q: How do you measure COE success? ▼
Key metrics include:
  • Number of active makers and apps in production
  • Compliance rate (% of apps with business justification)
  • Mean time to environment provisioning
  • DLP policy violation incidents over time
  • Orphaned resource count trend
  • Estimated ROI / hours saved via automation
  • Maker community engagement (training attendance, champion count)

Governance Components

Compliance Process

  • Emails makers of apps shared with 20+ users (or 1+ group) with no business justification
  • Apps not published in 60 days flagged for re-publish
  • High-impact apps require a mitigation plan attachment
  • Business Process Flows (BPFs) guide admins: Validate → Assess Risk → Approve
  • Developer Compliance Center — maker app to submit justifications & support details

Inactivity & Quarantine

  • Apps/Flows inactive 6 months → approval sent to owner to keep or delete
  • Ignored approvals → manager notified via Cleanup Old Objects App
  • App quarantine after X days of non-compliance (default: 7 days)
  • Quarantined app: access revoked, maker emailed, must submit compliance details
  • Admin reviews, completes risk assessment, manually releases quarantine

Teams Environment Governance

When a Dataverse for Teams environment is created, an adaptive card is sent to the team owner requesting a business justification. No justification within 7 days → environment deleted. No apps/flows after 90 days → deleted. (This process is also now available natively in PPAC.)

Orphaned Resource Management

What happens when a maker leaves the org?

  • CLEANUP flow runs weekly and checks all makers against Azure AD / M365 Users
  • Resources owned by users no longer in the directory are flagged as orphaned
  • "Request Orphaned Objects Reassigned" flow sends Teams adaptive card to the former owner's manager
  • Manager chooses: take ownership, assign to someone else, delete, or email the list to themselves
  • If no manager found, admin receives a list of unresolvable orphaned objects

Interview Q&A — Chapter 2

Q: Walk me through setting up the COE Starter Kit from scratch. ▼
  1. Create a dedicated COE host environment (not Default) with Dataverse
  2. Assign a service account with Power Platform Admin role
  3. Download the kit from aka.ms/CoEStarterKitDownload
  4. Import Core Components solution first
  5. Run the CoE Setup Wizard to configure env variables, email settings, communication groups
  6. Enable and run Sync flows to populate inventory
  7. Set up Power BI Dashboard connecting to Dataverse data
  8. Import Governance Components; configure compliance thresholds
  9. Import Nurture Components; configure welcome email
Q: How does the COE Starter Kit complement PPAC? ▼
PPAC provides native, fully-supported capabilities: environment management, Managed Environments, tenant-level analytics, DLP policies, Pipelines. The COE Starter Kit adds custom capabilities not yet in PPAC: bulk permission changes, maker community workflows, ROI calculations, granular compliance processes with approval chains, orphaned resource management, and cross-departmental Power BI reports with org data overlaid. Best practice: start with PPAC, then layer in the Starter Kit for gaps.
Q: What is the DLP Impact Analysis app and when would you use it? ▼
It lets admins draft proposed DLP policy changes and see which existing canvas apps and cloud flows would break before applying the change. Use it when: tightening policies (e.g. moving a connector to Blocked), adding new connectors to a policy, or during policy consolidation. You can then proactively contact impacted makers before enforcing the change, reducing business disruption.
Q: How does app quarantine work? ▼
The "Admin | Quarantine non-compliant apps" flow runs daily. If an app has had compliance details requested but not submitted for more than X days (default 7), the app's quarantine flag is set to Yes. The "Admin | Set app quarantine status" flow then revokes user access to the app and emails the maker explaining why. The maker submits compliance details via the Developer Compliance Center. An admin then reviews, completes a risk assessment, and manually releases the quarantine from Power Platform Admin View.

Default Environment Governance

  • Every tenant has exactly one Default environment
  • All licensed users are Environment Makers by default
  • Highest sprawl risk — must be governed proactively
  • Apply strict DLP limiting to standard connectors only
  • Use Default Environment Routing (Managed Env) to redirect makers to personal developer environments
  • Consider disabling canvas app creation in Default via tenant setting for mature COEs

Environment Lifecycle Stages

  • Request: Maker submits via COE Maker Command Center
  • Review: Admin evaluates business case, connector needs, capacity
  • Provision: Created with DLP applied; Managed Environments enabled on PROD
  • Active: Monitored via COE sync, capacity alerts, compliance flows
  • Decommission: Inactivity check → approval → deletion after 90 days no use

Environment Capacity Planning

Capacity Types to Know

  • Dataverse Database: 10 GB tenant base + 5 GB per qualifying D365/PP license
  • Dataverse File: 20 GB tenant base + 2 GB per qualifying license
  • Dataverse Log: 2 GB tenant base
  • Power Automate: Cloud flow run limits based on license type (per user per day)
  • AI Builder Credits: Pooled at tenant level; shared across all environments
  • COE Capacity Alerts flow monitors actual usage vs. admin-approved thresholds per environment

Interview Q&A — Chapter 3

Q: How would you design an environment strategy for a 5,000-user enterprise? ▼
I'd propose a hub-and-spoke model:
  • COE Hub environment — hosts Starter Kit, shared connectors, reusable components library
  • Business Unit spokes — each BU gets DEV/UAT/PROD trio; Managed Environments on PROD
  • Default environment — locked to personal productivity, strict DLP, Default Env Routing enabled
  • Developer environments — one per maker via Developer Plan; completely isolated
  • All new environments provisioned via automated request workflow, not ad hoc
  • Naming convention enforced at creation; tagging by BU, region, lifecycle stage
Q: What is Default Environment Routing and why is it important? ▼
Default Environment Routing (a Managed Environments feature) automatically redirects makers who would normally create resources in the Default environment to their own personal developer environment instead. This prevents sprawl in Default, gives each maker an isolated safe space to experiment, and preserves Default for genuine personal productivity use. Critical for mature COEs with many makers — without it, the Default environment becomes an unmanageable junkyard.
Q: How do you handle an environment provisioning request? ▼
Using the COE environment request process: maker submits request via Maker Command Center (purpose, estimated users, connector needs, business case). Admin reviews in COE Admin Environment Request app — checks DLP compatibility, capacity, and business justification. On approval, the "Env Request | Create approved environment" flow provisions the environment and automatically applies the correct DLP policy. The request tracks an expiry date; the cleanup flow handles unused environments after expiry with warning emails before deletion.

Managed Envts

What are Managed Environments?

A suite of premium admin capabilities built into PPAC allowing admins to govern Power Platform at scale — more control, less effort, better insights. Can be enabled on any environment type. Included with standalone Power Apps, Power Automate, Copilot Studio, Power Pages, and Dynamics 365 licenses.

Full Feature Set

Control & Security Features

  • Limit Sharing — Cap how widely apps can be shared (e.g., max 20 users or groups only)
  • IP Firewall — Restrict environment access to trusted IP ranges
  • IP Cookie Binding — Block cookie replay attacks
  • Customer Managed Key (CMK) — Bring your own encryption key for Dataverse data at rest
  • Lockbox — Microsoft engineer access requires explicit customer approval
  • Virtual Network Support — Route traffic through organisation's Azure VNet
  • Conditional Access per app — Individual Azure AD CA policies per canvas app
  • Control allowed apps — Allow-list specific apps in an environment
  • Masking Rules — Mask sensitive column data in specific contexts

Visibility & Enablement Features

  • Weekly Usage Insights — Digest email to admins on environment activity
  • Solution Checker Enforcement — Block solution import if checker fails at configured threshold
  • Maker Welcome Content — Custom guidance shown when makers first sign in to Power Apps
  • Default Environment Routing — Redirect new makers to personal developer environments
  • Data Policies Visibility — See all DLP policies applying to the environment
  • Export to App Insights — Telemetry to Azure Application Insights for custom alerting
  • Pipelines in Power Platform — Native managed CI/CD for solution deployments
  • Extended Backup — Longer backup retention beyond standard 7 days
  • Environment Groups — Apply settings to groups of environments at once

Interview Q&A — Chapter 4

Q: When would you enable Managed Environments on an environment? ▼
Always enable on Production environments. Consider enabling on UAT for ALM Pipeline support and Solution Checker enforcement. Key triggers: need IP Firewall for sensitive data environments, Sharing Limits for regulated industries, App Insights integration for custom monitoring, or native Pipelines for ALM. Note: Managed Environments is a premium entitlement — confirm license coverage before enabling at scale (included in standalone PP/D365 licenses, NOT in M365-seeded use).
Q: What is Solution Checker enforcement and why is it a governance tool? ▼
Solution Checker is a static analysis tool that checks Power Platform solutions for quality, reliability, and security issues (hardcoded URLs, deprecated APIs, performance anti-patterns). When enforcement is enabled on a Managed Environment, solutions fail to import if they don't pass the checker at the configured severity threshold (low/medium/high/critical). This is a proactive quality gate — it prevents poorly built or risky solutions from reaching production without a developer even needing to remember to run it manually.
Q: What is the Limit Sharing feature and why is it important for governance? ▼
Limit Sharing caps the number of users/groups an app in a Managed Environment can be shared with (e.g., max 20 individual users; group sharing optional). This is proactive enforcement — the platform itself blocks over-sharing, not just a compliance email after the fact. Critical for regulated industries (finance, healthcare) where data access scope must be tightly controlled. Without it, a maker could accidentally share a sensitive business app with the whole organisation.

DLP Policies & Security
Connector tiers · Policy scope · Advanced features · Dataverse security model
DLP

DLP in one sentence

DLP policies control which connectors can work together in Power Apps and Power Automate — preventing data leakage between incompatible services by grouping connectors into Business, Non-Business, and Blocked tiers.

Connector Classification Tiers

Business Approved

  • Approved enterprise data connectors
  • SharePoint, Dataverse, M365, Teams, Azure
  • Can share data within this group
  • Cannot mix with Non-Business group

Non-Business Restricted

  • Consumer / personal-use connectors
  • Twitter, personal OneDrive, Gmail
  • Can share data within this group
  • Cannot mix with Business group in same app/flow

Blocked Denied

  • Completely unavailable in the environment
  • Flows/apps using these connectors break immediately
  • Use for high-risk or disallowed connectors
  • Cannot be used in any app or flow

DLP Policy Scope

Tenant-Level Policies

  • Apply to ALL environments or selected environments
  • Set by Global Admin or Power Platform Admin only
  • Cannot be overridden by environment admins
  • Only one "all environments" policy possible per tenant
  • Use for org-wide baseline (e.g., block dangerous connectors everywhere)

Environment-Level Policies

  • Set by environment admin or tenant admin
  • Apply to a specific environment only
  • Most restrictive policy wins when combined with tenant policy
  • Use for BU-specific requirements (e.g., HR-only BambooHR connector)
  • An environment-level policy cannot unblock what a tenant policy blocks

DLP Advanced Features

Connector Endpoint FilteringRestrict which specific URLs/endpoints a connector can call (e.g., only allow SharePoint at specific on-prem URL). Not detectable by DLP Impact Analysis app.

Power Platform Security Model (Layers)

AZURE AD / ENTRA ID Authentication · Conditional Access · MFA · Guest access policies ↓ TENANT LEVEL Tenant DLP policies · License assignment · Tenant isolation ↓ ENVIRONMENT LEVEL Environment DLP · Environment Admin role · IP Firewall (Managed Env) Managed Environment settings · Sharing limits · Solution Checker enforcement ↓ DATAVERSE LEVEL Security roles (table/column/row level) · Business Units Teams (sharing groups) · Column-level security profiles · Hierarchy security ↓ APP / FLOW LEVEL App sharing (Can View / Can Edit / Owner) Connection references · Run-only users for flows · Per-app Conditional Access

Dataverse Security — Key Concepts

Security Roles

  • Collection of privileges: Create / Read / Write / Delete / Append / AppendTo / Assign / Share
  • Applied at Business Unit or user level
  • Privilege scope: User / Business Unit / Parent BU / Organisation
  • Built-in: System Administrator, System Customizer, Basic User, Environment Maker
  • Always create custom roles for least-privilege access

Column & Row Level Security

  • Column-level security profiles: Restrict read/write/create on specific columns
  • Row-level via record sharing: Share specific Dataverse records with users or teams
  • Owner team vs. Access team: Owner teams inherit security roles; access teams for temporary record-level sharing
  • Hierarchy security: Managers can access direct reports' records

Interview Q&A — Chapter 5

Q: A business unit needs to use the Twitter connector but your tenant DLP blocks it. How do you handle this? ▼
First, understand the business case. If legitimate (e.g., social media monitoring): (1) Create an environment-specific policy for that BU's dedicated environment allowing Twitter in Non-Business tier — BUT the tenant policy still wins. (2) If tenant policy has Twitter as Blocked, I need to use a DLP policy exemption to exclude that environment from the blocking tenant policy. Document the exemption with: business justification, approver name, review frequency, and risk acceptance sign-off. Communicate the change to affected makers via DLP Impact Analysis first.
Q: How would you design a DLP strategy for a financial services company? ▼
  • Tenant baseline policy: Block all consumer connectors (Gmail, Dropbox, social media), raw HTTP connector, unvetted third-party connectors
  • Business group: SharePoint, Dataverse, Azure services, M365, approved internal APIs
  • Environment-specific policies: Add only the connectors needed by that specific BU (e.g., SAP connector only in Finance environment)
  • Custom connector governance: All custom connectors default to Blocked; require security review + classification before enabling
  • Endpoint filtering: For any allowed HTTP connector, restrict to approved internal API gateway endpoints only
  • DLP for desktop flows: Restrict PAD modules to approved automation types in regulated environments
  • Review quarterly using DLP Impact Analysis before any changes
Q: What is the difference between tenant-level and environment-level DLP policies? ▼
Tenant-level policies are created by Global/Power Platform Admins and apply across all or selected environments — environment admins cannot override them. Environment-level policies are set by environment admins and only apply to that single environment. When both exist, the most restrictive combination applies — a connector blocked at tenant level cannot be unblocked at environment level. This layered approach lets you set an org-wide baseline at tenant level, then add BU-specific restrictions at environment level.

ALM

Solution Types

Managed Solutions ✅ (for PROD/UAT)

  • Used in production and UAT environments
  • Cannot be directly edited — prevents unmanaged customisation
  • Exported from unmanaged in DEV; version-controlled
  • Can be upgraded cleanly; deleted without residue
  • Non-negotiable best practice for all deployments

Unmanaged Solutions 🔧 (DEV only)

  • Used in development environments only
  • Fully editable — where makers and devs build
  • Exported and packed as managed before deploying to UAT/PROD
  • Never import unmanaged into production — creates ungovernable customisations
  • Should be stored in source control as unpacked YAML

Interview Q&A — Chapter 6

Q: Why should you never import an unmanaged solution into production? ▼
Unmanaged solutions cannot be cleanly deleted — their components merge into the unmanaged layer of the environment and leave residual customisations even after the solution is removed. This creates customisation debt that's very hard to remediate. Additionally, unmanaged components can be edited directly in production, bypassing the ALM pipeline entirely, leading to environment drift and breaking the single source of truth in source control. This is a critical governance failure in regulated environments.
Q: What is the difference between Environment Variables and Connection References? ▼
Environment Variables store configuration values that differ per environment — e.g., SharePoint site URL is different in DEV vs. PROD, or an Azure Key Vault reference for a secret. You set the value during import without touching the solution logic.

Connection References abstract the actual authenticated connection a flow or app uses. In DEV you map it to your dev account's connection; in PROD you map it to a service account connection. The solution holds the reference name, not a hard-coded connection ID — so the deployment pipeline just maps the reference to the appropriate connection for each environment at import time.
Q: How do you set up an end-to-end DevOps pipeline for Power Platform? ▼
  1. Install Power Platform Build Tools extension in Azure DevOps
  2. Create a service connection using a service principal (SPN) with Power Platform Admin permissions
  3. Build pipeline (CI): Export solution from DEV → Unpack with pac CLI → Commit unpacked YAML to repo → PR triggers solution checker
  4. Release pipeline (CD): Pack solution from YAML → Deploy managed to UAT → Run Solution Checker → Approval gate → Deploy managed to PROD
  5. Use pipeline variables / variable groups for env-specific connection reference mappings
  6. Optionally: Run Power Apps Test Engine tests between UAT and PROD stages
  7. Notify stakeholders on deployment completion via Teams / email action

Env Groups

Think of Environment Groups as "folders" for your environments

Instead of configuring each environment one-by-one (error-prone at scale), you group environments into logical collections and apply rules that lock down settings across all environments in the group simultaneously. A rule published at group level cannot be overridden by environment admins — only a tenant admin can change it.

Key constraint: Environment Groups can only contain Managed Environments. Each environment can belong to only ONE group. Groups cannot be nested.

How Environment Groups Work (Simple Mental Model)

TENANT ADMIN creates Environment Group: "Production Environments" ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────── ┌─ Environment Group: "Production Environments" ────────────────────┐ │ Rules published: │ │ ✅ Sharing Limit = 50 users max │ │ ✅ Solution Checker = Critical errors blocked │ │ ✅ Backup Retention = 28 days │ │ ✅ Maker Welcome Content = "Welcome to PROD, read policies first"│ │ │ │ Environments in this group (all auto-inherit rules): │ │ ├── HR-PROD ← locked, can't change sharing limit locally │ │ ├── FIN-PROD ← locked, can't change sharing limit locally │ │ └── OPS-PROD ← locked, can't change sharing limit locally │ └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘ If HR admin tries to change sharing limit in HR-PROD → blocked by group rule Only TENANT ADMIN can update the rule at group level

4 Key Use Cases for Environment Groups

1. Personal Productivity Sandbox Group

  • Combine with Default Environment Routing — every new maker auto-gets a developer env placed in this group
  • Apply rules: restrict agent sharing (so in-progress bots don't leak), add maker welcome content
  • Keeps Default environment clean while every maker has their own governed space
  • Result: Maker isolation like "each person has their own OneDrive"

2. Production Environments Group

  • Group all PROD environments across all BUs
  • Rules: Solution Checker = critical block, Sharing Limit = tight, Backup = 28 days, Maker Welcome = read policy first
  • Benefit: No environment admin can accidentally loosen PROD governance
  • Changes only by tenant admin — enforced change control

3. AI Pilot / Copilot Group

  • Create a dedicated group for environments testing Copilot features
  • Enable AI features only in this group — PROD group has AI turned off until ready
  • Controlled, phased AI rollout across the org
  • Move environments between groups as confidence grows

4. Regional Compliance Group

  • Global enterprises: separate groups for North America, Europe (GDPR), APAC
  • Apply region-specific compliance rules at group level
  • Environments within each region auto-inherit local regulations-aligned settings
  • No per-environment configuration needed — set once, apply to hundreds

Environment Routing + Groups = Fully Automatic Governance

The golden combination

Set up Default Environment Routing to point to a specific Environment Group. Result: when any new maker signs up and tries to create in Default, the platform automatically creates their personal developer environment AND places it in the correct group — inheriting all group rules immediately. No admin action needed. The maker gets a governed space; the admin gets control. This is the closest Power Platform gets to fully automated environment governance.

Interview Q&A — Chapter 9

Q: How are Environment Groups different from just applying Managed Environment settings individually? ▼
Individual ME settings require you to configure each environment separately — at 100+ environments, this becomes unmanageable and leads to configuration drift (environments slowly diverge). Environment Groups let you define settings once at the group level and automatically push them to all member environments. Critically, group rules lock the settings so local admins cannot override them — enforcing consistency. It's the difference between manually setting the speed limit on each road vs. having a centralized speed control system.
Q: What happens to an environment's settings when you remove it from a group? ▼
When an environment is removed from a group, it retains the last configuration applied by the group's rules, but those settings become unlocked. The local environment admin can then modify them freely. This is important — removing from a group doesn't reset settings to factory defaults; it just unlocks them for local management. This means you can move environments between groups without losing their current configuration.

Dataverse

Why this matters for an architect

Dataverse security is multi-layered and often misunderstood. Getting it wrong means either too much access (data breach risk) or too little (users can't do their job). As an architect, you design the security model before any app is built — because changing it later is painful and risky.

The 4 Levels of Roles in Power Platform

Environment-levelEnvironment Admin, Environment MakerPPAC → Environment → SettingsCreate/manage resources in one env (no Dataverse data access)

CRITICAL — The "Admin Tax" trap architects must know

A Power Platform Admin at tenant level does NOT automatically get Dataverse data access in any environment. They must ALSO be assigned the System Administrator Dataverse security role in each specific environment they need to access data in. Many architects get caught out by this — the tenant admin can manage environments but can't see any data without the explicit Dataverse role assignment.

Security Privileges — The 8 Building Blocks

Core CRUD + Append

  • Create — Make new records
  • Read — View records
  • Write — Edit existing records
  • Delete — Remove records
  • Append — Link to another record (e.g. contact to account)
  • Append To — Allow others to link TO this record

Sharing Privileges

  • Assign — Change the owner of a record
  • Share — Grant access to another user without changing ownership
  • These two are the most powerful — they can escalate access beyond the security role
  • Restrict Share privilege on sensitive data tables (e.g. HR, Payroll)

Privilege Scope (Access Levels)

  • User — Only own records
  • Business Unit — Own + BU members' records
  • Parent: Child BU — Own BU + all child BUs
  • Organisation — All records in the environment
  • Start with User scope; expand only when needed

Business Units — The Org Hierarchy in Dataverse

ROOT BUSINESS UNIT (= tenant name) │ ├── Sales BU │ ├── North Sales Team │ └── South Sales Team │ ├── HR BU │ └── Payroll Team │ └── Operations BU Key rules: • Every user belongs to exactly one Business Unit • Security role privileges are scoped to the user's BU by default • "Business Unit" privilege scope = all records owned by users in the SAME BU • "Parent: Child BU" scope = user's BU + all BUs beneath it in the hierarchy • Moving a user to a different BU changes their data access scope • Root BU users with "Org" scope = see everything

Teams in Dataverse — 3 Types

Team TypeWhat it isKey BehaviourBest for
Owner TeamA team that can be assigned as the OWNER of recordsTeam members inherit the team's security roles; records owned by team visible to all membersShared record ownership — e.g. a case owned by "Support Team" not a specific person
Access TeamDynamic team — records don't have a permanent owner teamMembers get temporary access to specific records via Access Team template; no security role inheritanceScenario where specific people need short-term access to specific records (e.g. a deal team for one opportunity)
Azure AD Group TeamTeam backed by an Azure AD security group or M365 groupMembership auto-syncs with Azure AD group; assign security roles to team, all group members inherit themLarge orgs managing access via Azure AD — best for scale (add user to AD group → automatically gets Dataverse access)

Column-Level Security (Field-Level Security)

What it is

  • Restricts access to specific columns (fields) on a table — regardless of table-level security role
  • A user may have Read access to the Contact table, but a CLS profile can hide the "Salary" column from them
  • Controlled via Field Security Profiles — independent of security roles
  • Applies to: Custom columns, and specific system columns enabled for field security
  • Three settings per column: Allow Read / Allow Update / Allow Create

When to use it

  • PII fields (passport number, SSN, salary, bank account)
  • Sensitive business data (margin, deal discount %)
  • Internal-only fields on otherwise publicly visible records
  • Compliance requirements (GDPR, HIPAA) requiring column-level restriction
  • Design tip: Enable column security EARLY — adding it later to existing data doesn't retroactively protect already-read data

How Dataverse Determines Record Access (the algorithm)

DATAVERSE ACCESS CHECK — in order: 1. Does the user own the record? → YES = Access granted (if security role allows) ↓ NO 2. Is the user in the same BU as the owner? → YES (if BU scope privilege) = Access granted ↓ NO 3. Is the user in a parent BU of the owner's BU? → YES (if Parent BU scope) = Access granted ↓ NO 4. Does the user have Org-level scope on the table? → YES = Access granted ↓ NO 5. Is the record shared directly with the user? → YES = Access granted ↓ NO 6. Is the user in a team that owns/has access? → YES = Access granted ↓ NO 7. Does the user have a hierarchy security position → YES = Access granted above the owner? ↓ NO → ACCESS DENIED

Interview Q&A — Chapter 10

Q: How would you design the Dataverse security model for a Sales org with 3 regional teams? ▼
  • Create 3 Business Units: North Sales, South Sales, West Sales (all under Root BU)
  • Create an Owner Team per region (e.g. "North Sales Team")
  • Custom security role: "Sales Rep" — Read own + BU Accounts, Create/Write own Opportunities, Read BU Contacts
  • Custom role: "Sales Manager" — all of above + Read Parent:Child BU (sees all 3 regions' records)
  • For sensitive deal margins: enable Column-Level Security on Margin field; create a profile "Sales Manager Only" — only managers can see it
  • For deal teams: use Access Teams so multiple reps can collaborate on one opportunity without permanently sharing ownership
  • Backend integrations: use Application User (service principal) with a minimal custom role — NOT System Administrator
Q: What is the difference between an Owner Team and an Access Team in Dataverse? ▼
Owner Team: A team that can be assigned as the owner of records. All team members inherit the team's security roles and can see all records owned by the team. Best for scenarios where "the team owns the work" — e.g. a support case owned by "Tier 1 Support Team" not a specific agent.

Access Team: A dynamic team where individual records are shared with specific users on a per-record basis using an Access Team Template. Members get access to that specific record only, not the team's security roles. Best for short-term collaboration on specific records — e.g. a major deal team for one opportunity where an analyst needs temporary access.
Q: A Power Platform Admin says they can't see any data in a Dataverse environment. Why? ▼
This is a very common confusion. A Power Platform Admin (tenant-level role) grants the ability to manage environments, set DLP policies, and view environment settings — but it does NOT grant Dataverse data access. To access data in a specific Dataverse environment, the admin must also be assigned the System Administrator security role within that specific environment. These are two completely independent permission layers: one is an Azure AD admin role, the other is a Dataverse table-level security role.

Interview Q&A — Chapter 11

Q: When would you use a Patch vs an Upgrade for a managed solution? ▼
Patch: Small, urgent fix that only changes specific components (like fixing a bug in one flow or updating a form field). Fast to deploy. Downside: patches accumulate as layers and create complexity over time.

Upgrade: Major release that replaces the entire solution. Rolls up all patches, can delete unused components, and delivers a clean version. Use it at the end of a sprint/feature set. Prefer Upgrade + "Stage for Upgrade" option to see impact before committing.

General best practice: use Patches for hotfixes, Upgrades for planned releases. Never accumulate more than 2-3 patches without rolling into an Upgrade.
Q: What is a solution dependency and why does it matter in ALM? ▼
A solution dependency means Solution B cannot be installed without Solution A (the base). For example, a "BusinessLogic" solution that uses tables from a "DataModel" solution has a dependency on DataModel. Consequences: (1) You must deploy DataModel first. (2) You cannot delete/uninstall DataModel while BusinessLogic is still installed. (3) If DataModel has a bug that requires deletion of a column, BusinessLogic must be removed first. Design principle: keep your foundation solutions (data model) very stable with few changes — because everything depends on them.

For example, if you have 3 machines running overnight batch processes, you need 3 Process licenses — regardless of how many flows run on each machine.

Design-Time vs Runtime DLP Enforcement

Design-Time (Maker experience)

  • When a maker tries to add a blocked connector to an app or flow, they cannot save
  • Error message shown immediately: "This connector is blocked by your organisation's data policy"
  • Prevents new violations from being created
  • Applies to Power Apps, Power Automate, Copilot Studio maker studio
  • Effective immediately when policy is saved

Runtime (Existing apps & flows)

  • Existing apps/flows that were built BEFORE a policy change may still run temporarily
  • DLP policy change triggers a cascade process that evaluates all existing resources
  • Violating resources are placed in suspended/quarantine state — they stop running
  • Connections to fully blocked connectors are disabled
  • Latency: up to 24 hours in large tenants before full enforcement takes effect

Advanced Connector Policies (ACP) — The Power Tool

What is ACP and when do you need it?

Advanced Connector Policies go beyond simple Business/Non-Business/Blocked tiers. With ACP you can: restrict which specific actions of a connector are allowed (e.g., SharePoint "Get Items" allowed but "Delete Items" blocked), filter by endpoint URL (e.g., HTTP connector can only call api.contoso.com), and govern MCP server endpoints for AI agents. ACP is for organisations that need granular control beyond what standard DLP tiers provide — typically financial services, healthcare, or orgs with strict data residency requirements.

Interview Q&A — Chapter 14

Q: What is the difference between standard DLP policies and Advanced Connector Policies (ACP)? ▼
Standard DLP policies classify entire connectors into Business/Non-Business/Blocked tiers — it's an all-or-nothing control per connector. ACP provides action-level and endpoint-level granularity: you can allow "Get Items" on SharePoint but block "Delete Items"; or allow the HTTP connector only to endpoint https://api.contoso.com and block all other URLs. ACP is the right choice when you can't block an entire connector (business need exists) but need to restrict dangerous operations within it. ACP does NOT support virtual connectors — those have their own governance path.
Q: You've just changed a DLP policy to block the Twitter connector. How soon does it take effect for existing flows using Twitter? ▼
Not immediately. When a DLP policy is saved, a cascade process evaluates all existing resources in the affected environments. In most cases this takes under 1 hour, but in tenants with thousands of apps and flows, it can take up to 24 hours for full enforcement. During this window, existing flows may still run. The connection to Twitter will be disabled once the cascade completes, and any flow using it will fail at runtime with a broken connection error. Best practice: warn makers with 2 weeks' notice, use DLP Impact Analysis beforehand to identify all affected flows, and plan the change for a low-traffic period.
Q: What are virtual connectors and why should a COE architect know about them? ▼
Virtual connectors are not real APIs — they appear in DLP policies as on/off switches for platform features, primarily for Copilot Studio governance (e.g., turning off specific chatbot capabilities like web search or file upload). They cannot be governed by ACP (advanced connector policies). This is important for COE architects because: (1) Copilot Studio governance requires understanding virtual connectors to control what AI agents can do. (2) Microsoft is evolving virtual connectors into their own dedicated governance capability separate from DLP entirely — so stay updated on this changing area. (3) If you need to govern Copilot/AI features, this is the current mechanism.

Pillar 1: Reliability

5 Design Principles

  • Design for business requirements — Define RTO and RPO; not every app needs 99.99% uptime

  • Design for recovery — Test your DR plan; document every restore step

Pillar 4: Performance Efficiency

Key performance facts for PP architects

Q: What is delegation in Power Apps and why is it a critical performance concern? ▼
Delegation means the filter/query is processed by the data source SERVER rather than downloaded to the client first. Without delegation, Power Apps downloads data to the client — but only the first 500-2000 records (configurable, max 2000). If your table has 50,000 records and you have a non-delegable filter, you search ONLY the first 2000 rows — silently returning incorrect results with no error shown to users. Architects must: (1) Always use Dataverse for large-scale data (widest delegation support), (2) Always verify delegation in the formula bar (yellow triangle = warning), (3) Never ship an app with delegation warnings on business-critical query paths.

Tenant Isolation — Stop Cross-Tenant Data Flow

What it is and why it matters

  • Controls whether your users can connect PP apps to data in OTHER Azure AD tenants
  • Does NOT affect normal Azure AD access outside Power Platform
  • Inbound isolation: Block users from other tenants connecting to YOUR data via PP connectors
  • Outbound isolation: Block YOUR users connecting to other tenants' data
  • Whitelist specific trusted tenants (e.g., a partner org) while blocking everything else

Exchange Rules — Stopping Email Abuse from Power Automate

The risk

  • Office 365 Outlook = standard connector — CANNOT be DLP-blocked
  • A maker could build a flow sending 10,000 external emails (blast, phishing, data ex-fil)
  • Power Automate injects identifying SMTP headers into every email sent
  • Header: x-ms-mail-application: Microsoft Power Automate
  • Header: x-ms-mail-environment-id: <env-guid>
  • This lets Exchange rules identify and block PP-originated emails specifically

Exchange rules to implement

  • Block external email blasts: If x-ms-mail-application contains "Power Automate" + recipient is external + source env = Default env ID → block
  • Block auto-forwarding: Block forward operations to external addresses from PP in Default env
  • Exception allowlist: Approved flows/apps (by GUID) exempted from the block rules
  • Best practice: Move ANY app/flow sending external email to a dedicated governed environment — not Default
