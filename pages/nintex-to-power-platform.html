A stakeholder discussion guide covering the retirement of legacy Nintex capabilities and recommended Microsoft Power Platform replacement patterns, with a focus on Power Pages for external and anonymous submission scenarios.

Executive Summary

Legacy Nintex workflow and forms capabilities — specifically those built on the classic SharePoint Nintex Workflow and Nintex Forms products — are reaching end-of-support. Organisations that have built business-critical processes on these platforms face a clear strategic decision: migrate proactively to modern Microsoft Power Platform alternatives, or risk disruption, security vulnerabilities, and vendor unsupportability.

This guide provides decision-makers and technical leads with a structured framework for understanding the impact, evaluating replacement options, and planning a phased migration to Microsoft Power Platform — with particular focus on scenarios involving external users, anonymous submissions, and attachment handling, where Power Pages is the recommended platform capability.

It is an opportunity to re-evaluate how your organisation captures, routes, approves, and acts on information — and to build those processes on a unified, supported, governed Microsoft platform that integrates natively with your existing M365 and Azure investments.

Current-State Problem Statement

Many organisations built extensive business process automation on Nintex Workflow for SharePoint and Nintex Forms during the SharePoint 2010–2019 era. These tools were market-leading at the time but were tightly coupled to classic SharePoint infrastructure, on-premises deployments, and legacy browser models. As Microsoft has shifted to cloud-first, modern SharePoint, and the Power Platform ecosystem, these Nintex products have become misaligned with the direction of both Microsoft and the broader market.

Why Legacy Nintex Processes Cannot Simply Be Moved

⚠️
Redesign, Not Lift-and-ShiftUnlike infrastructure migrations, workflow and form migrations cannot be automated. Nintex Workflow XML definitions do not translate to Power Automate flow definitions. Nintex Forms ASPX markup does not translate to Power Apps or Power Pages. Each process must be analysed, redesigned, and rebuilt using modern Power Platform patterns. Some will be simpler than their Nintex equivalents; others will be more capable.

Why Change Now

The question is not whether to migrate — it is when and how. Organisations that delay face compounding risk: increasing technical debt, security vulnerabilities in unsupported products, reduced vendor support responsiveness, and the growing complexity of migrating a larger inventory of processes in a shorter window. Early movers gain a longer runway, lower cost per migration, and the ability to pilot and learn before high-risk processes are tackled.

Market and Microsoft Strategy Signals

  • 📌Microsoft has invested over $3B in Power Platform — it is a strategic platform, not a feature. Nintex is a third-party niche tool competing in Microsoft's primary automation space.
  • 📌Power Automate now has feature parity with and in many areas exceeds classic Nintex Workflow capabilities, including approvals, HTTP triggers, AI Builder integration, and Copilot-assisted flow creation.
  • 📌Power Pages (formerly Power Apps Portals) has matured significantly with built-in Dataverse integration, Copilot-assisted design, anonymous access, and enterprise security controls.
  • 📌Microsoft's Copilot/AI strategy is deeply embedded in Power Platform — future capabilities (AI-generated workflows, smart forms, process intelligence) are exclusive to the Microsoft stack.

The Hidden Impact: Invisible Workflow Failures

🚨
Workflows Don't Always Break LoudlyOne of the most dangerous aspects of Nintex retirement is that workflows may not fail with an error — they may simply stop sending notifications, stop escalating, or stop updating records, with no alert to the process owner. Approvals that were expected to complete in 2 days may silently stall for weeks. Discovering these failures often requires an incident or audit finding.

Quantifying the Risk

🔴 Security Risk Unpatched Nintex engine vulnerabilities on unsupported SharePoint versions. No security fixes after end-of-support.
🟠 Compliance Risk Loss of audit trail integrity for regulated processes (HR, Finance, Legal). Cannot demonstrate approval chains during audits.
🟡 Operational Risk Silent workflow failures cause delays in business-critical approvals. Manual workarounds increase cost and introduce human error.
🔵 Vendor Risk Nintex support tickets deprioritised for legacy products. Licence renewal for deprecated software with no product roadmap.
🟢 Knowledge Risk Nintex skills are declining in the market. Staff who built original solutions leave; documentation is absent; no one can maintain what exists.
🟣 Integration Risk SharePoint REST APIs and legacy SOAP endpoints used by Nintex are being sunset. Integrations to third-party systems routed through Nintex break silently.

Impact on External-Facing Processes — Special Attention Required

The most acute short-term risk for many organisations is the failure of external-facing Nintex forms — portals where vendors, clients, or the public submit documents, complaints, or requests. These processes often:

  • Have no authentication (anonymous submissions) — making SharePoint-only replacements unsuitable
  • Require file attachment handling (PDFs, images, signed documents) — which adds complexity to the replacement design
  • Are SLA-bound — a failed external submission portal may breach contractual or regulatory commitments
  • Have no fallback — external users cannot access SharePoint directly to submit manually
🌐
Power Pages Is the Answer for External SubmissionsPower Pages (formerly Power Apps Portals) is Microsoft's purpose-built platform for secure, externally accessible web portals with Dataverse integration, anonymous access, attachment support, and enterprise-grade security controls. Replacement Solution Options

Microsoft Power Platform provides a set of complementary, integrated tools that together cover every capability Nintex provided — and more. No single tool replaces Nintex entirely; the right combination depends on the scenario, audience, and data requirements of each process.

The Power Platform Toolkit

Power Automate — Workflow & Process Automation (Primary Nintex Workflow Replacement)

What it replaces: Nintex Workflow for SharePoint. Sequential approvals, parallel branching, timed escalations, notifications, and system integrations.

Key capabilities:

  • Cloud flows triggered by SharePoint events, form submissions, HTTP requests, scheduled timers, or manual triggers
  • Approval actions with multi-stage, parallel, and sequential patterns; configurable escalation and time-out logic
  • 800+ pre-built connectors to M365, Azure, Salesforce, ServiceNow, Dynamics 365, and more
  • Exception handling, retry policies, and detailed run history for audit and debugging
  • Copilot-assisted flow creation: describe the process in plain English and Power Automate generates a draft flow
  • Desktop flows (RPA) for automating legacy desktop applications with no API

Best for: Multi-step approvals, notification routing, SharePoint list automation, cross-system data synchronisation, scheduled batch operations.

Licensing: Included in Microsoft 365 E1/E3/E5 with standard connectors. Premium connectors (Dataverse, HTTP, SAP) require Power Automate Premium (£12.30/user/month or per-flow plan).

Power Pages — External Web Portals & Submission Sites (Primary replacement for external-facing Nintex Forms)

What it replaces: External-facing Nintex Forms, especially scenarios with anonymous access, attachment uploads, and external user authentication.

Key capabilities:

  • Branded, publicly accessible web portals with no SharePoint licence requirement for external users
  • Anonymous (unauthenticated) access for public-facing submission forms
  • Authenticated access via Azure AD B2C, local identity, or social identity providers
  • File attachment support to Dataverse (notes/annotations entity)
  • Dataverse forms and lists rendered as web pages with full CRUD capability
  • Liquid templating for dynamic content; Copilot-assisted page building
  • OOB integration with Power Automate for post-submission workflows
  • Web application firewall (WAF), DDoS protection, and enterprise security controls

Best for: Vendor document intake, complaint/incident submission, public-facing service requests, anonymous feedback portals, external onboarding forms.

Licensing: Power Pages Authenticated users: £3.20/user/month (pay-per-user) or £100/site/month (anonymous capacity). See Section 7 for full licensing detail.

Power Apps — Custom Internal Applications & Complex Forms (Internal Nintex Forms replacement)

What it replaces: Complex internal Nintex Forms with conditional logic, calculated fields, dynamic sections, and multi-screen workflows for licensed users.

Key capabilities:

  • Canvas Apps: pixel-precise form and application design for internal users; can embed in Teams, SharePoint, or standalone
  • Model-driven Apps: structured applications driven by Dataverse schema; ideal for complex data models (CRM-style)
  • SharePoint-connected forms: replace SharePoint list forms with Power Apps custom forms (zero additional licence cost in M365)
  • Offline capability for field workers with no internet connectivity
  • AI Builder integration for document processing, object detection, and prediction models
  • Power Apps for Teams: lightweight apps embedded directly in Teams channels

Best for: Complex internal forms with multi-step logic, mobile field apps, SharePoint list form replacements, Dataverse-backed business applications.

Licensing: Power Apps Premium: £16.10/user/month. SharePoint form customisation via Power Apps: included in M365 at no extra cost.

Microsoft Forms — Simple Surveys & Questionnaires

What it replaces: Simple single-page Nintex Forms used for surveys, polls, and data collection without complex logic.

Key capabilities:

  • Simple drag-and-drop form builder; no training required
  • Branching logic for conditional questions
  • Anonymous and authenticated response modes
  • Automatic response collection in Excel; Power Automate integration for response triggers
  • Embedded in SharePoint pages, Teams tabs, and emails

Best for: Quick surveys, simple questionnaires, event registrations, feedback collection. Not for complex multi-stage forms, attachment uploads, or structured data entry.

Licensing: Included in all Microsoft 365 plans at no additional cost.

Dataverse — Enterprise Data Platform (Underpins Power Pages, Power Apps, and Power Automate)

What it replaces: SharePoint lists used as Nintex Workflow data stores. Provides a proper relational data store with business rules, security roles, and audit capabilities.

Key capabilities:

  • Relational tables with relationships, lookups, and calculated columns
  • Row-level security via Dataverse security roles — no manual SharePoint permission management
  • Built-in audit log for every row create/update/delete — essential for compliance
  • Business rules, real-time workflows, and plug-ins for server-side logic
  • OData API, Power BI connector, and Azure Synapse Link for data integration and reporting
  • File and image attachments stored natively (notes entity)

Best for: Any Power Pages or Power Apps solution requiring structured, auditable, secure data storage. Required for Power Pages.

Licensing: Included with Power Apps Premium, Power Pages, and Dynamics 365 licences. Dataverse storage is pooled per tenant.

SharePoint + Power Automate — Document-Centric Workflows

What it replaces: Nintex Workflow for document approval, review routing, and version-controlled document management on SharePoint libraries.

Key capabilities:

  • SharePoint document libraries with version history, check-out/check-in, content types, and approval status columns
  • Power Automate flows triggered on file upload, metadata change, or manual button
  • Approval actions with email and Teams notification; comments captured in approval history
  • Conditional logic for multi-path routing (e.g., values over £10k go to Director, under go to Manager)
  • Approval outcome written back to SharePoint column for reporting and compliance

Best for: Contract review, policy approval, document sign-off, content publishing workflows where the primary artefact is a file.

Licensing: Power Automate standard connectors included in M365. Premium connectors require Power Automate Premium.

Common External Submission Scenarios — Power Pages Design Patterns

🏭 Vendor Document Intake Portal

Scenario: Vendors must submit compliance certificates, insurance documents, and onboarding paperwork on a recurring basis. Previously a Nintex Form on an extranet SharePoint site.

Power Pages design:

  • Power Pages site with vendor authentication (Azure AD B2C local account — vendors register themselves)
  • Multi-step form: vendor details → document type selection → file upload → submission confirmation
  • Dataverse table stores submission record; notes entity stores the attachment
  • Power Automate: acknowledge receipt → route to procurement team → notify if incomplete → track status
  • Vendor can return to portal to check submission status (using authenticated session)

Key advantage: Vendors do not need a corporate M365 account. Self-service registration via B2C. Full audit trail in Dataverse.

📢 Complaint & Incident Submission Portal

Scenario: Customers or members of the public can submit complaints or safety incidents. Submissions must be anonymous and auditable. Previously a Nintex Form on a public-facing SharePoint site.

Power Pages design:

  • Power Pages site with fully anonymous access enabled at the table permission level
  • Single-page form with CAPTCHA to prevent bot submissions
  • Optional file upload (photo evidence, supporting documents) via Dataverse notes
  • Reference number generated and displayed on confirmation page (Dataverse auto-number column)
  • Power Automate: categorise by type → assign to case owner → SLA timer started → notify team
  • No personally identifiable information required for truly anonymous submissions (configurable)

Key advantage: Fully anonymous, CAPTCHA-protected, publicly accessible. No external user licence required for submitters.

🧑‍💼 External Onboarding & Service Request Portal

Scenario: New clients, contractors, or partner organisations complete onboarding forms, submit required documentation, and track request status. Previously managed via Nintex Forms on an extranet.

Power Pages design:

  • Authenticated portal (local identity or AAD B2C) — users create an account and have a persistent session
  • Multi-step onboarding wizard: organisation details → contact details → document uploads → agreement acceptance → submit
  • Status dashboard page showing submission progress (using Dataverse data list web part)
  • Power Automate handles internal review routing, document validation, and status updates back to Dataverse (visible to submitter on return visit)
  • Email notifications at each stage driven by Power Automate

Key advantage: Submitters can return to check status without needing a corporate account. All data in Dataverse enables CRM-style case management internally.

Security, Compliance & Governance Considerations

Moving from Nintex to Power Platform is not just a feature swap — it is a transition to a governed, enterprise-grade platform with significantly more sophisticated security controls. Understanding these controls early ensures that the replacement architecture is not only functionally equivalent but materially more secure and auditable than what it replaces.

Data Security

Security AreaNintex Legacy PosturePower Platform Posture
Data storageSharePoint lists — SharePoint permission model; no row-level securityDataverse — table-level and row-level security via security roles; independent of SharePoint permissions
File attachment securityAttachments in SharePoint document libraries — governed by SharePoint permissionsAttachments in Dataverse notes — governed by Dataverse table permissions and owner-level security
External access controlSharePoint extranet access — managed via SharePoint external sharing settings; complex to governPower Pages — explicit table permissions define what external users can read/write; zero-trust by default
Anonymous accessAnonymous access to SharePoint is broadly disabled in most tenants for security reasonsPower Pages anonymous access is controlled at table permission level — granular per-table control
Audit logSharePoint audit log — limited; not row-level for workflow state changesDataverse audit — every field change tracked with user, timestamp, old value, and new value; exportable
EncryptionSharePoint: encryption at rest (Microsoft-managed key) and in transit (TLS 1.2+)Dataverse: same encryption at rest + customer-managed key (CMK) option for regulated sectors

Power Pages–Specific Security Controls

  • 🔐Table permissions (zero-trust by default): External users have no access to Dataverse tables unless explicitly granted via Power Pages table permission records. Even authenticated portal users cannot see data they are not explicitly permitted to access.
  • 🔐Web Application Firewall (WAF): Power Pages sites are protected by Azure Front Door with WAF enabled by default. DDoS protection, bot detection, and OWASP Top 10 mitigations are included.
  • 🔐CAPTCHA on anonymous forms: OOB CAPTCHA integration prevents automated bot submissions on anonymous-access forms without custom development.
  • 🔐Content Security Policy (CSP): Configurable CSP headers prevent XSS and injection attacks on portal pages.
  • 🔐HTTPS enforced: All Power Pages sites enforce HTTPS with TLS 1.2+. Custom domains include automated SSL certificate provisioning.
  • 🔐IP restriction: Power Pages can be restricted to specific IP ranges for authenticated portals used by known external partners.

Phased Migration Roadmap

  1. Wave 0 — Foundation (Weeks 1–6) — Complete Nintex inventory. Establish Power Platform environments (Dev/Test/Prod). Configure DLP policies. Set up CoE Starter Kit. Identify pilot processes for Wave 1. Train core team on Power Automate and Power Apps fundamentals. Output: Governance baseline, complete inventory, Wave 1 candidates identified.
  2. Wave 1 — Quick Wins: Low-complexity Internal Flows (Weeks 6–12) — Migrate 3–5 simple sequential approval workflows with no external access and no attachments to Power Automate. Replace 2–3 basic SharePoint list forms with Power Apps SharePoint form customisations. Validate deployment pipeline and ALM process. Output: Proof-of-concept in production; team builds confidence; pipeline validated.
  3. Wave 2 — Medium-Complexity Internal Processes (Weeks 12–20) — Migrate multi-stage approval workflows with escalation, parallel branching, and Teams notifications. Replace complex internal Nintex Forms with Power Apps Canvas or Model-driven apps backed by Dataverse. Migrate document approval workflows to SharePoint + Power Automate pattern. Output: Core internal process portfolio migrated; Dataverse adoption established.
  4. Wave 3 — External-Facing Processes: Power Pages (Weeks 18–28) — Assess all external-facing Nintex Forms. Design and build Power Pages sites for each external scenario (vendor intake, complaint submission, onboarding portals). Configure Azure AD B2C for authenticated external users where needed. Test WAF, CAPTCHA, anonymous access, and attachment upload end-to-end. Output: External process portfolio migrated; Power Pages in production with security controls validated.
  5. Wave 4 — Complex & Integrated Processes (Weeks 26–40) — Migrate complex Nintex workflows with third-party system integrations (ERP, ITSM, CRM). Redesign processes that relied on legacy Nintex-specific features (state machine workflows, scheduled workflows). Introduce AI Builder for document processing and RPA for legacy system integration where APIs are unavailable. Output: Full Nintex portfolio migrated. Nintex licences sunset.
  6. Wave 5 — Optimisation & Decommission (Weeks 38–48) — Nintex server decommission (if on-prem). Nintex licence termination. Power Platform adoption reporting via CoE. User training and change management completion. Establish ongoing Power Platform governance operating model. Output: Nintex fully retired. Power Platform operating model in steady state.
✅
Migration Is Also SimplificationMost organisations discover during Wave 1 that their legacy Nintex workflows are significantly over-engineered for the actual business need. Many approval workflows built in 2015 had complex branching logic to compensate for Nintex's limited notification capabilities. Power Automate's modern approval action handles 80% of these scenarios in 5 steps. Use migration as an opportunity to simplify, not replicate.

FAQ — Business & Technical Audiences

Business Questions

Q: Do all our Nintex workflows need to be migrated at the same time?

No. The recommended approach is a phased wave migration starting with low-complexity, low-risk processes. High-criticality processes migrate later once the team has built confidence in the new platform. Nintex workflows that are rarely used or no longer serve a business need should be decommissioned, not migrated.

Q: Will our users need retraining when forms change?

Some retraining will be needed, but Power Platform tools are designed to be more intuitive than classic SharePoint forms. Power Apps Canvas forms look like modern mobile apps. Power Pages portals look like professional websites. The change management effort is primarily about communicating where to go, not how to use a complex new tool.

Q: How long will the migration take?

A full migration typically takes 9–18 months depending on the inventory size, process complexity, and resource availability. Simple organisations with fewer than 20 workflows can complete in 6 months. Complex enterprise deployments with 100+ workflows, external portals, and third-party integrations may take 18–24 months. Early planning with a complete inventory enables accurate scoping.

Q: What happens to business data stored in Nintex-linked SharePoint lists?

SharePoint list data remains in SharePoint — Nintex workflows and forms do not own the data, they just read and write to SharePoint lists. The migration decision is: does this data move to Dataverse, stay in SharePoint, or get archived? For processes moving to Power Apps or Power Pages, structured data should move to Dataverse for proper governance. For document-centric processes staying in SharePoint, the list data can remain.

Q: Can external vendors still submit without a Microsoft account?

Yes — this is one of Power Pages' primary strengths. Power Pages supports anonymous access (no login at all) and local identity registration (vendors create a portal-specific account with just an email and password — no Microsoft account required). Azure AD B2C also supports social identity logins (LinkedIn, Google) for scenarios where vendor organisations have those accounts.

Q: Will there be additional licensing costs?

It depends on your current M365 licensing and which tools you need. Power Automate standard connectors are included in M365 E1/E3/E5. If you need premium connectors (Dataverse, HTTP) or Power Apps Premium or Power Pages, additional per-user or per-site licences are required. However, these costs should be weighed against the savings from terminating Nintex licences and the risk reduction value of migrating off end-of-support software.

Technical Questions

Q: Can we use a tool to automatically migrate Nintex workflows to Power Automate?

No reliable automated migration tool exists for Nintex Workflow to Power Automate. The two platforms have fundamentally different architectures — Nintex uses XML-based workflow definitions on SharePoint; Power Automate uses JSON-based cloud flow definitions with a different connector model. Migration requires analysis, redesign, and rebuild for each workflow. Some simple linear approval workflows may be recreatable in 1–2 hours; complex state-machine or parallel branch workflows may take days.

Q: Can Power Automate replicate Nintex's state machine workflow capability?

Power Automate does not have a native visual state machine designer. Complex state-dependent processes are better modelled using Dataverse status columns combined with conditional logic in Power Automate flows, or using Power Apps Model-driven apps with business process flows (BPF) — which provide a visual stage-based progress model. In most cases, redesigning the process as a simple approval chain with clear stages is preferable to replicating a Nintex state machine exactly.

Q: Does Power Pages support large file uploads?

Power Pages supports file attachments via Dataverse notes (annotation entity). The default attachment size limit is 5MB but this is configurable up to 128MB in Dataverse system settings. For very large files (videos, large engineering documents), the recommended pattern is to use Azure Blob Storage with a SAS token for direct upload, with the SAS URL or blob reference stored in Dataverse — bypassing the Dataverse attachment size limit entirely.

Q: Can Power Pages handle multiple file uploads in a single submission?

Yes. Power Pages supports multiple file attachments on a single form submission. Each attachment is stored as a separate note record linked to the parent Dataverse record. You can configure the form to allow multiple files, specify accepted file types (e.g., PDF, DOCX, JPG only), and set maximum file count. Power Automate can then process each attached file as part of the post-submission workflow.

Q: How do we handle on-premises system integration from Power Automate?

Power Automate connects to on-premises systems via the On-Premises Data Gateway. This is a lightweight agent installed on a server in your network that acts as a secure bridge between Power Automate cloud flows and on-premises SQL Server, file shares, web services, or SAP systems. For APIs exposed over the internet, no gateway is required — the HTTP connector or the relevant premium connector handles the integration directly.

Q: Does Power Pages work with our existing Azure AD tenant?

Yes. Power Pages integrates with Azure AD for internal user authentication (employees can log in with their corporate credentials). For external users who are not in your Azure AD, you configure Azure AD B2C as the identity provider — this is a separate Azure resource but integrates with your existing AAD tenant. Azure AD B2C handles external identity registration, multi-factor authentication, and social login without requiring external users to be added as guests to your corporate directory.
