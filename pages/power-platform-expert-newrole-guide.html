What Success Looks Like
Outcomes, KPIs, and maturity targets
⚡ Quick Read

Success in three sentences

Governance is in place before makers hit problems. ALM is consistent and repeatable, not heroic. The team builds faster and with more confidence because standards are clear, not because someone reviews every line.

Success Outcomes by Horizon

  • Tenant audit complete — environments, DLP, existing apps/flows documented
  • Quick wins identified and at least 2 delivered (naming standards, DLP review)
  • Key stakeholders mapped and initial trust established
  • ALM baseline agreed — solution structure, unmanaged vs managed decision
  • CoE install decision made (install, enhance, or operate without toolkit)
  • First governance conversation with the platform admin held
  • DLP policy design reviewed and updated if needed
  • Environment strategy documented and agreed with delivery lead
  • At least one pipeline (Power Platform or Azure DevOps) operational for a real delivery stream
  • Citizen developer intake process published (even if simple)
  • Solution packaging standard in place and being followed
  • At least one governance checkpoint embedded in the delivery rhythm
  • Full ALM pipeline operational Dev → Test → UAT → Prod
  • CoE monitoring reports running (even basic ones)
  • Team upskilled on solutions, connection references, environment variables
  • Standards documented and published internally
  • Citizen developer first cohort onboarded with training completed
  • Governance charter or operating model v1 published
  • Managed Environments assessed and rollout decision made
  • Regular governance review cadence established (monthly/quarterly)
  • App inventory visible and owned — no "ghost apps" in production
  • Deployment is automated and repeatable, not manual
  • Makers self-serve with documented process and templates
  • Leadership trust established — you are the go-to for all PP governance questions

Governance Maturity Model

L1Ad HocNo DLP, no ALM, no standards. Anything goes. High risk.
L2ControlledSome rules exist but inconsistent. Known admin. Basic DLP.
L3StandardisedDLP in place, ALM followed, naming standards, intake process active.
L4GovernedCoE operating, pipelines automated, monitoring dashboards, CoE cadence.
L5OptimisedMeasurable outcomes, maker community thriving, continuous improvement cycle.
🗣️ PM Talking Point

When asked "how is governance going?" — show the maturity model. Tell them where you started (L1/L2) and where you are now. Quantify: "We went from no DLP policy to two policies covering all environments in 3 weeks. We now have full ALM across Dev/Test/UAT."

🏗️ Architect Note

Don't aim for L5 on day one. A sustainable jump from L1→L3 in 90 days is excellent. L4 within 6 months is architect-level delivery. Document the maturity state at intake and revisit quarterly.

Governance Fundamentals
DLP, environments, admin model, standards, and compliance
⚡ Quick Read

Governance in one sentence

Governance is the set of policies, processes, and guardrails that allow an organisation to benefit from Power Platform at scale — without creating data risk, technical debt, or unmanaged sprawl.

The Governance Pillars

🌍

Environment Strategy

Right environment for the right purpose. Default, Developer, Sandbox, UAT, Production — each with clear ownership and DLP coverage.

🔒

DLP / Data Policies

Connector grouping rules that control what data sources can combine in apps and flows. First line of data exfiltration defence.

👥

Roles & Responsibilities

Tenant Admin vs Environment Admin vs Maker vs User. Clear ownership prevents governance gaps and duplicate effort.

📊

Monitoring & Visibility

CoE kit reports, admin centre analytics, capacity, and inventory. You cannot govern what you cannot see.

🏗️

ALM & Solutions

Solution-based packaging, managed downstream, no direct prod edits. ALM is governance for the development lifecycle.

📋

Standards & Naming

Naming conventions, solution naming, flow naming, connector documentation — makes the tenant auditable and maintainable.

DLP Strategy Summary

Critical DLP policies control which connectors can be used together in the same app or flow. Every environment needs at least one active policy. See the DLP Policies Deep Dive Guide for full depth.

ConceptWhat It MeansArchitect Guidance
Business groupConnectors approved for enterprise data use (SharePoint, Dataverse, Teams)Only trusted, approved connectors belong here
Non-business groupConnectors approved for non-sensitive use (Planner, MSN Weather)Low-risk connectors that don't touch business data
Blocked groupConnectors not permitted in this policy scopeStart here for consumer connectors (Gmail, Dropbox, Twitter)
Tenant-wide policyApplies to all environments unless overridden by a stricter env policyUse for baseline "floor" coverage — block all consumer connectors
Environment policyApplies only to a specific environment — can be more restrictiveUse for sensitive environments (Prod, Finance, HR)
Most restrictive winsWhen multiple policies apply, the combination that is most restrictive is enforcedDesign policies in layers — tenant floor + env-specific refinement
💡 DLP Quick Design

Tenant policy: Business = {SharePoint, Dataverse, Teams, Outlook, OneDrive}. Block = {Gmail, Dropbox, Twitter, Facebook, Instagram}. Non-business = everything else.
Production env policy: Business = {SharePoint, Dataverse, Teams, SQL Server}. Block = everything else. This layered approach gives you a tenant safety net AND environment-specific hardening.

  • Risk tier assigned — Low / Medium / High
  • Environment assigned based on tier

  • DLP design documented and shared with admin

🏗️ Architect Note

Governance is not only about the CoE Starter Kit. The kit is an accelerator — it provides a set of pre-built flows and apps for visibility. Governance itself is the people, process, standards, and communication model around it. A mature tenant can have excellent governance with minimal tooling if the processes and ownership are clear.

Managed Environments

Advanced Managed Environments is a premium feature that adds governance controls to specific environments. Requires per-app or per-user P2 licensing, or Managed Environment add-on.

✅ When to use Managed Environments

  • Production environments with sensitive data
  • Environments requiring maker welcome message and usage insights
  • Weekly digest email for admins on tenant activity
  • Solution checker enforcement on import
  • Sharing limits (e.g. max 20 people per canvas app)

❌ When NOT to start with Managed Environments

  • Developer environments (over-controls individual sandboxes)
  • Organisations without premium licensing budget
  • As a substitute for proper DLP policies — it's additive, not a replacement

CoE / Enablement
Center of Excellence structure, Starter Kit, operating cadence
⚡ Quick Read

What a CoE actually is

A CoE is not a tool installation. It is a people + process + tooling model that enables an organisation to gain value from Power Platform sustainably. The CoE Starter Kit is a Microsoft open-source accelerator — a useful starting point, not the whole operating model.

CoE vs. No CoE

✅ With a CoE

  • Visibility into who is building what, in which environment
  • Standards published and accessible — makers don't guess
  • Intake process — business ideas are assessed and matched to the right tooling
  • Training and community — makers improve over time
  • Governance cadence — regular review, proactive risk management
  • Escalation path — makers know who to call when they're stuck

❌ Without a CoE

  • Apps proliferate in the default environment with no oversight
  • No one knows what's in production or who owns it
  • Personal account connections in critical flows — break on staff changes
  • No DLP strategy — any connector can be used with any other
  • Citizen developers blocked by IT or fully uncontrolled — neither is good
  • No handover process for apps built by departing staff

Core CoE Team Structure

👑

Executive Sponsor

Senior leader who champions Power Platform. Provides air cover for governance decisions and budget for licensing/tooling.

🏛️

CoE Lead / PP Expert

Runs the CoE — that's you. Owns the operating model, standards, cadence, and maker community.

⚙️

Platform Admin

Implements governance decisions in the Admin Centre. Manages environments, DLP, licensing, and monitoring.

🌟

Champions Network

Power users and enthusiastic makers in each business unit. Multipliers for standards adoption and peer support.

🛡️

Security / Compliance Rep

Ensures DLP and data governance aligns with organisational security policy and audit requirements.

CoE Starter Kit — What It Is and Isn't

ComponentWhat It DoesImportant Caveat

Nurture ComponentsMaker onboarding, community apps, training challenge, maker portalOptional. Very useful for large-scale citizen developer programmes.

The CoE Starter Kit requires careful setup — it uses multiple Cloud Flows and apps that need service principal or admin credentials. Treat it as a reference implementation, not a plug-and-play install. Always validate the latest Microsoft Learn setup guide before deploying. Plan for a dedicated CoE environment with Dataverse.

CoE Intake Process (Lightweight)

Even a simple intake process is better than none. The goal is to ensure new Power Platform work is visible, assessed, and correctly resourced before the first line of no-code is written.

1. Request Submission

Day 0

  • Maker or business owner submits request via form (Microsoft Form, SharePoint list, or CoE portal)
  • Captures: business need, data involved, users affected, expected outcome

2. Triage & Risk Assessment

Within 2 business days

  • CoE Lead reviews submission
  • Assigns risk tier: Low (personal productivity) / Medium (team workflow) / High (sensitive data, external users)
  • Recommends: Power Platform / pro-dev / existing tooling

3. Environment & DLP Assignment

Same day as triage

  • Assigns appropriate environment based on risk tier
  • Confirms DLP policy coverage for connectors needed
  • Provides solution template and naming guide

4. Build & Review

During development

  • Maker builds in assigned environment following standards
  • Architecture review for Medium/High tier apps before deployment
  • Solution checker run before any promotion

KPIs for CoE Success

📈

Active Makers

Number of makers who have built at least one app or flow in the last 90 days. Target: growing month-on-month.

🏗️

Apps in Solutions

% of production apps packaged in a solution. Target: 100% in production, 80%+ overall.

📋

Intake Compliance

% of new apps/flows that went through the intake process. Target: 95% within 90 days of CoE launch.

🌱

Champion Network

Number of active champions across business units. Target: at least one per major department.

🎯 Interview / Stakeholder Talking Point

When asked "what is a CoE?" — say: "A Center of Excellence is an operating model — not a tool installation. It combines governance policies, enablement processes, community building, standards publication, and regular cadence to ensure Power Platform delivers value at scale without creating risk. The CoE Starter Kit is a useful accelerator, but the real CoE is the people and process around it."

Citizen Developer Program
Onboarding, risk tiers, approval model, and support boundaries
⚡ Quick Read

Citizen development in one sentence

Citizen development works in enterprise when guardrails enable rather than block — clear risk tiers, approved environments, intake process, training, and escalation to pro-dev teams when needed.

What Citizen Development Means in a Controlled Enterprise

Citizen development is not "anyone can build anything anywhere." In a well-governed enterprise it means: authorised business users can build low-risk productivity apps and flows within approved environments, using pre-approved connectors, following published standards, with a clear escalation path for complex or high-risk use cases.

When NOT to Use Power Platform

⚠️ Governance Guardrail — Redirect to Pro-Dev

Citizen developer tools are not appropriate for every use case. Escalate to professional development when any of these apply:

  • Complex data transformations or large-scale data migrations
  • External-facing portals requiring custom authentication or branding beyond Power Pages
  • Performance-sensitive applications with hundreds of concurrent users
  • Regulated processes requiring full audit trail, digital signature, or legal non-repudiation
  • Custom API development or low-level system integrations
  • Machine learning model training (use Azure ML or AI Foundry, not AI Builder for model training at scale)

ALM Foundations
Solutions, versioning, managed vs unmanaged, deployment standards
⚡ Quick Read

ALM in one sentence

Solutions are the unit of ALM in Power Platform. Everything that matters must be in a solution, exported as managed to downstream environments, and deployed via a pipeline — never edited directly in production.

Why ALM Matters

🔁

Repeatability

The same solution can be deployed to any environment reliably — not manually recreated each time.

🕵️

Traceability

Every change has an origin — source control shows who changed what and why. Auditors love this.

↩️

Rollback

Managed solutions can be deleted to restore a prior state. Direct prod edits cannot be rolled back cleanly.

🔒

Production Integrity

Managed solutions prevent direct edits in production — the only way to change is through a proper deployment.

Solution Concepts

ConceptDefinitionArchitect Guidance
SolutionContainer for Power Platform components — apps, flows, tables, choices, env vars, connection referencesEvery production workload must live inside a solution. No exceptions.
Unmanaged solutionEditable solution — used in development environmentsDev only. Never deploy an unmanaged solution to Test, UAT, or Prod.
Managed solutionRead-only solution deployed downstream — components cannot be edited in that environmentAlways deploy managed to Test/UAT/Prod. Enforces production integrity.
Default solutionSystem solution that holds all components not explicitly added to a custom solutionTreat as a warning sign — components here are not properly managed.
LayersMultiple managed solutions can be layered on top of each other in one environmentAvoid deep layering (3+ layers). Complex to manage, debug, and roll back.
PublisherMetadata attached to a solution — defines the custom prefix for all componentsUse a consistent, org-specific publisher prefix. Never use the default "cr" prefix in production.

Solution Segmentation Strategy

💡 Solution Segmentation Principle

Avoid one giant monolithic solution for an entire application portfolio. Separate by: (1) Domain — HR vs Finance vs Operations, (2) Layer — Core Data (Dataverse tables) separate from UI (apps/flows), (3) Deployment frequency — infrastructure components (tables, choices) deploy less often than UI components.

Solution TypeContainsDeploy Frequency
Core / Data layerDataverse tables, choices, columns, relationshipsLow — schema changes are infrequent and risky
Business Logic layerBusiness rules, plugins, cloud flows (core automation)Medium — changes with business requirements
UI / App layerCanvas apps, model-driven app customisationsHigh — most frequent iteration target
Configuration layerEnvironment variables, connection references, site mapLow to medium — env-specific values, not logic
Security layerSecurity roles, field-level security profilesLow — permissions rarely change

Managed vs. Unmanaged — The Critical Rules

✅ DO — Managed Downstream

  • Export as managed before deploying to Test/UAT/Prod
  • Store the managed .zip in source control or pipeline artifact
  • Import managed — the environment will enforce read-only on components
  • Rollback by deleting the managed solution if needed
  • Use deployment settings file to pass env-specific values at import time

❌ DON'T — Unmanaged Downstream

  • Do not export unmanaged and import into production
  • Do not let developers make direct edits in production environments
  • Do not deploy from the "default solution" — components are unmanaged and uncontrolled
  • Do not skip solution checker before import — quality gate matters
  • Do not layer 4+ managed solutions on the same environment — creates invisible merge issues

Versioning Strategy

Semantic versioning is the standard: Major.Minor.Patch.Build

Version PartWhen to IncrementExample
MajorBreaking change, complete redesign, major new feature set1.x.x → 2.0.0
MinorNew feature, new screen, new flow added1.2.x → 1.3.0
PatchBug fix, minor label change, small formula update1.2.3 → 1.2.4
BuildPipeline build number — auto-incremented by CI/CD1.2.4.100

Healthy ALM Principles

  • ✓All production components live inside a named, versioned solution with a custom publisher
  • ✓Dev environment uses unmanaged — all downstream environments receive managed
  • ✓Source control holds the exported unmanaged solution .zip (or unpacked source files)
  • ✓No one edits directly in UAT or Production — ever
  • ✓Environment variables used for all environment-specific values (URLs, IDs, keys)
  • ✓Connection references used for all connector-based components in solutions
  • ✓Solution checker run before every import to Test or above
  • ✓Deployment settings file defines env-specific values for each target environment
  • ✓Release notes and version history maintained with each deployment
🏗️ Architect Note — Current Team Situation

If the team currently has separate pipelines per form + unmanaged solutions + no premium licensing + connection/env variable challenges, the immediate priorities are: (1) Move to a shared solution per domain, (2) Fix connection references — every flow in a solution needs one, (3) Define environment variables for all URLs and site addresses, (4) Export as managed before deploying downstream. These four steps alone will dramatically improve ALM discipline before any pipeline investment.

Deployment Architecture
Release promotion, deployment patterns, and target-state model

Recommended Enterprise Deployment Architecture

Deployment Flow — Step by Step

1. Develop in Dev

Unmanaged solution, free iteration

  • All changes made in Dev environment only
  • Environment variables and connection references configured for Dev
  • Solution committed to source control on completion of feature

2. Export and Package

Via pipeline or manually

  • Export unmanaged for source control
  • Export managed for downstream deployment
  • Run Solution Checker — resolve all critical/high issues
  • Generate deployment settings file for target environment

3. Deploy to Test

Automated — no approval

  • Pipeline imports managed solution + deployment settings to Test
  • Automated smoke tests run (if defined)
  • Developer validates key scenarios

4. Promote to UAT

Approval gate

  • Same managed package deployed to UAT
  • Business owner / tester validates acceptance criteria
  • Sign-off captured (Teams approval, email, or pipeline gate)

5. Deploy to Production

Change control gate

  • CAB approval or change ticket raised and approved
  • Pipeline deploys to Production in defined maintenance window
  • Post-deployment validation — check app loads, key flows run
  • Release notes published and stakeholders notified

Environment Variables & Connection References
The two config mechanisms every ALM-based deployment depends on
⚡ Quick Read

Why they exist

Environment variables replace hardcoded URLs, IDs, and values so the same solution works in Dev, Test, and Prod without modification. Connection references allow flows and apps in a solution to use different actual connections per environment without changing the solution itself. Both are mandatory for professional ALM.

Environment Variables

TypeUse ForExample
TextURLs, site names, email addresses, named identifiersSharePoint site URL, list name
NumberThresholds, timeouts, limitsApproval timeout hours = 48
BooleanFeature flags, toggle switchesEnableEmailNotifications = true
JSONComplex configuration objectsEnvironment-specific API endpoint config object
Data sourceSharePoint site, Dataverse table referenceSharePoint site for a specific environment
SecretAPI keys, passwords — stored in Azure Key VaultExternal API key (never stored in plain text)

Key Rules for Environment Variables

  • ✓Add env var to the solution (not just created in the environment — it must be in the solution to deploy)
  • ✓Set a current value per environment — the solution holds the schema, the environment holds the value
  • ✓Reference in flows using the environmentVariables function — never hardcode the fallback value in production
  • ✓Use deployment settings JSON to supply values at import time for automated pipelines
  • ✓For secrets, always use the Azure Key Vault type — never put credentials in a text env var
// In Power Fx (Canvas App) — referencing an env var
LookUp(EnvironmentVariableValues, SchemaName = "acme_SharePointSiteURL").Value

// In Cloud Flow — use the environment variable directly
// Add "Get environment variable value" action or reference @{variables('EnvVarName')}

Connection References

A connection reference is a solution component that acts as a pointer to an actual connection. When a flow or app uses a connection reference, the actual connection can be different in each environment — the solution doesn't need to change.

ConceptWhat It Means
Connection reference (schema)Lives in the solution — names the connector type (e.g. "SharePoint")
Actual connectionLives in the environment — the real authenticated connection (owned by a user or service principal)
Mapping at importDuring import, the connection reference is mapped to the actual connection for that environment via deployment settings
Service account connectionsProduction flows must use service principal or service account connections — not personal accounts
🚨 Critical Anti-Pattern

Flows in production owned by or connected via personal user accounts will break when that user changes their password, leaves, or has their licences changed. Always use a service account (licensed functional account) or service principal for production Cloud Flows.

Canvas Apps & Cloud Flows Best Practices
Enterprise deployment patterns, ownership, and common mistakes

Canvas App Enterprise Deployment Standards

  • ✓Canvas app must be inside a solution — not created directly in an environment without a solution
  • ✓App name follows naming standard: [Org]-[Function]-App
  • ✓All data connections use connection references — not direct connections
  • ✓All environment-specific URLs/IDs accessed via environment variables
  • ✓App published and solution exported (managed) before deploying downstream
  • ✓App description includes: purpose, owner, version, last reviewed date
  • ✓Delegation enabled for all data sources where collections exceed 500 records
  • ✓Error handling on all network/connector calls (IfError, IsBlank)
  • ✓App accessibility checked — screen reader labels, colour contrast, keyboard navigation
  • ✓Solution Checker run before import — no critical or high issues in production

Cloud Flow Enterprise Deployment Standards

  • ✓Flow inside a solution — never build "my flows" (non-solution flows) for team/production use
  • ✓All connections via connection references in the solution
  • ✓Flow owned by or running as a service account — not a personal account
  • ✓Flow name follows standard: [Org]-[Trigger]-[Action]-Flow
  • ✓Error handling: Scope actions with Configure run after set to handle failures
  • ✓Retry policy configured for connector actions where appropriate
  • ✓Run history retained — consider a separate logging flow or Dataverse logging table for critical flows
  • ✓DLP policy confirmed before adding connectors — Business + Non-Business combinations blocked
  • ✓Sensitive data not passed in flow names, email subjects, or logged to plain text

Packaging App + Associated Flows Together

💡 Packaging Rule

Canvas apps and their associated Cloud Flows must be in the same solution to deploy as a unit. If a flow is in a separate solution, it will not deploy atomically with the app and connection references will be unresolved on first import. Use the solution dependency resolution feature to identify cross-solution references before deploying.

Troubleshooting Broken Connections Post-Deployment

Service Account & Service Principal Strategy

🧑‍💼 Service Account (Functional User)

  • A licensed M365 account created for automation (e.g. svc-pp-flows@org.com)
  • Used for flows that require delegated permissions (SharePoint, Exchange)
  • Must have Power Automate Premium licence if using premium connectors
  • Password managed by IT with no individual ownership
  • MFA should use conditional access exclusion, not disabled

🔑 Service Principal (App Registration)

  • Azure AD app registration used for non-interactive authentication
  • Used for pipeline deployments (PAC CLI, Azure DevOps)
  • No licence required for deployment activities
  • Needs Power Platform role: Environment Maker or Admin in target envs
  • Client secret or certificate rotation must be managed

Dev / Test / UAT / Prod Model
Environment-specific standards, ownership, and what "good" looks like

Development Environment

  • Type: Sandbox (or shared Developer)
  • Solution state: Unmanaged — free iteration allowed
  • DLP: Permissive dev policy — allows more connectors for building/testing
  • Data: Synthetic/test data only — no real business data
  • Access: Dev team and makers — no business users
  • Deployment direction: Source of all changes — promote to Test only via pipeline
  • What good looks like: All in-progress work is in named solutions with custom publisher. Connection references and env vars configured. No components in the Default solution.

Test / QA Environment

  • Type: Sandbox
  • Solution state: Managed import only
  • DLP: Production-equivalent policy — mimics prod constraints
  • Data: Representative test data (anonymised production data if needed)
  • Access: Dev team and QA testers
  • Deployment direction: Only receives from Dev via pipeline — no manual edits
  • What good looks like: Automated deployment on Dev commit or sprint close. Solution Checker passes before import. Flows active and verified by dev team before handing to UAT.

UAT Environment

  • Type: Production-type or Sandbox (depending on data sensitivity)
  • Solution state: Managed import only
  • DLP: Production-equivalent or exact production policy
  • Data: Production-like data — may include real data with business user access
  • Access: Business owners, super-users, acceptance testers
  • Deployment direction: Receives from Test via pipeline, with business owner approval gate
  • What good looks like: Business stakeholder formally signs off before release to Prod. Acceptance criteria defined before UAT entry. Release notes provided to testers.

Production Environment

  • Type: Production
  • Solution state: Managed import only — no exceptions
  • DLP: Most restrictive policy — hardened
  • Data: Live business data — treat as highest sensitivity
  • Access: End users only — no developer access to edit components
  • Deployment direction: Receives from UAT via pipeline, with CAB / change approval
  • What good looks like: Zero direct edits. Deployment via approved change only. Service account connections. Monitoring active. Post-deployment validation documented. Rollback plan defined before every release.

What architects watch for, quick wins, and how to talk to stakeholders

Things Architects Watch For Immediately

  • ✓Apps and flows in the Default solution — sign of unmanaged, ungoverned assets
  • ✓Flows owned by personal accounts — break on staff changes
  • ✓No DLP policy on the Default environment — it's where everyone experiments by default
  • ✓Hardcoded URLs and SharePoint site names in flows — environment portability breaks immediately
  • ✓Multiple unrelated workloads in one environment — governance, licensing, and rollback are harder
  • ✓"We test directly in production" — the most common sign of L1/L2 maturity
  • ✓Large number of inactive/orphaned apps — no governance, no lifecycle policy
  • ✓No connection references in solutions — env-specific connections will break on deploy

How to Speak to Different Stakeholders

What they're really asking: Why does deployment take more than a few clicks?

Your answer: "We can deploy quickly when the pipeline is set up correctly — and setting it up right once takes 2 days but saves us hours every sprint. Rushing deployment without ALM means we can't roll back if something breaks in production, we can't trace what changed, and we can't manage concurrent environments. The investment in the pipeline pays back within two sprints."

Your answer: "We use three controls: (1) DLP policies that prevent mixing Business-approved connectors (SharePoint, Dataverse) with consumer connectors (Gmail, Dropbox) in the same app or flow, (2) Managed Environments which add sharing limits and weekly admin digests, (3) Tenant isolation settings to restrict cross-tenant data movement. These together provide the governance story for your audit requirements."

Your answer: "Our DLP policy places Gmail in the Non-Business group because it's a personal consumer email service. If it were in the same group as SharePoint or Dataverse, a flow could silently copy business data to a personal Gmail account — which is a data loss risk. If you have a genuine business need for an external email service, we can review adding an approved corporate email connector. What are you trying to achieve? There may be an approved connector that does the same job."

Your answer: "Direct production edits bypass every safety check we have — testing, UAT sign-off, change approval. They can't be rolled back cleanly. If something breaks after a direct edit, we can't identify the exact change or restore to the last known good state. The pipeline adds one extra step — test then deploy — but it gives you a production environment you can trust."

Your answer: "DLP policies in Power Platform are connector-aware data movement controls. They are complementary to — not a replacement for — Conditional Access, MIP/Purview labels, SharePoint permissions, and Defender. DLP prevents a user from accidentally or deliberately exfiltrating business data through an unapproved connector (e.g. sharing SharePoint data with a Dropbox flow). It doesn't replace identity-based access controls or encryption. The full governance story combines all of these layers."

Common Problems & Troubleshooting
Deployment failures, connection issues, DLP errors, and flow breaks

Troubleshooting Index

Symptom: Flow that was running fine now fails with a DLP policy violation error.

Cause: A new or updated DLP policy placed connectors used in this flow into different groups (Business vs Non-Business).

Diagnosis: (1) Open flow in Power Automate, check error message for the blocked connector. (2) Review DLP policies for the environment in Admin Centre. (3) Identify which policy changed.

Fix: Either: (a) Move the connector to the correct group in the DLP policy (requires admin), or (b) Rebuild the flow to only use connectors from the same DLP group, or (c) Use a different connector that is already in the required group. Do not simply move all connectors to Business group to "fix" the error — assess the data risk first.

Symptom: After importing a managed solution, flows show "connection not found" or "invalid connection".

Cause: Connection references in the solution are not mapped to actual connections in the target environment.

Fix: (1) In the target environment, open the solution and navigate to Connection References. (2) For each reference, click Edit and select the correct connection. (3) For automated pipelines, add the ConnectionReferences array to the deployment settings JSON file with the correct ConnectionId for the target environment. (4) Ensure the target connection is owned by a service account, not a personal account.

Symptom: Flow or app in Production is pointing to Dev SharePoint site or Dev API endpoint.

Cause: The env var current value was included in the exported solution, or deployment settings were not used at import.

Fix: (1) Immediately update the env var current value in Production to the correct value. (2) For the deployment pipeline, add EnvironmentVariables to the deployment settings JSON. (3) Remove the current value from the solution file before exporting — env var schema should not carry environment-specific values.

Cause: The solution depends on another solution component (Dataverse table, security role, custom connector) that does not exist in the target environment.

Fix: (1) Use Solution Checker to identify missing dependencies before export. (2) Ensure dependencies are in a separate "core" solution deployed before the dependent solution. (3) Deploy in the correct order: Core → Business Logic → UI.

Symptom: After deploying managed solution, flows in target environment are turned off.

Cause: This is expected behaviour — managed flows import as off to prevent unintended execution in a new environment.

Fix: (1) Manually turn on flows after import validation. (2) For automated pipelines, add a PAC CLI or PowerShell post-deployment step to activate flows: pac flow activate --flow-id {id} --environment {env} or use the Power Apps admin connector to activate programmatically.

Cause: Managed Canvas Apps import without sharing configuration. Sharing must be reconfigured in the target environment.

Fix: Share the app in Production with the appropriate security group. For Dataverse-based apps, ensure security roles are in the solution and assigned to users in the target environment. Consider a post-deployment runbook that documents sharing configuration per environment.

Common causes: (1) Service principal credentials expired. (2) Admin permissions not correctly configured for the CoE environment. (3) Throttling — too many API calls during initial sync. (4) CoE solution version outdated — Microsoft regularly releases updates.

Fix: (1) Check flow run history in the CoE environment for the specific failing flow. (2) Renew service principal credentials. (3) Re-run the CoE setup wizard. (4) Check the Microsoft Power Platform CoE Starter Kit GitHub for known issues and updates. (5) For initial sync throttling — stagger the flows or run outside business hours.

Anti-Patterns
What NOT to do — and why each pattern hurts

Why it's wrong: Components outside solutions cannot be moved, versioned, or deployed as a unit. They cannot be managed with pipelines. They accumulate as ungoverned technical debt that cannot be retrospectively added to managed solutions without risk of data loss.

Fix: Always create apps and flows inside a named solution. Create the solution first, then build inside it.

Why it's wrong: Breaks the ALM chain. Cannot be rolled back. Creates untraceable changes. Undermines the entire governance model. Creates a "shadow Dev" situation where Production drifts from source control.

Fix: Enforce managed environment settings that prevent direct editing. Train the team. Add a governance policy. Use the pipeline.

Why it's wrong: When the person leaves, changes their password, or loses a licence — every flow they own or that uses their connection breaks. This is one of the most common production incidents in Power Automate estates.

Fix: Use a service account or service principal. Migrate existing personal-owned flows to service account ownership. Audit regularly via CoE Starter Kit flow reports.

Why it's wrong: The solution only works in one environment. Deploying to Test or Prod requires manual editing of flows/apps after import — breaking the automated deployment model and introducing human error.

Fix: Environment variables for all URLs, list names, IDs, and configuration values. One solution — many environments.

Why it's wrong: DLP prevents connector cross-grouping. It does not govern identity, prevent sharing, enforce data retention, manage permissions, or enforce MFA. Treating DLP as a complete security solution leaves major gaps in the security posture.

Fix: Position DLP as one layer in a defence-in-depth model. Combine with Conditional Access, Purview labels, SharePoint permissions, and Managed Environments sharing controls.

Why it's wrong: A single global policy either over-blocks (Dev team can't work) or under-protects (Prod has the same permissions as Dev sandboxes). Different environments have different risk profiles and need different policies.

Fix: Layered DLP approach: permissive tenant floor + restrictive production environment policies. Dev policy allows broader experimentation. Prod policy locks to approved connectors only.

Why it's wrong: Unmanaged solutions allow direct editing in the target environment. This means Production can be changed without a pipeline. Rollback is not possible. Component ownership is unclear. This is the most common ALM anti-pattern in Power Platform teams.

Fix: Export as managed. Always. Train every developer. Enforce via pipeline — the pipeline only deploys managed packages.

Why it's wrong: The default "cr" publisher prefix creates anonymous component names. Components from different solutions become indistinguishable. Debugging, auditing, and refactoring become very painful.

Fix: Define a custom publisher with a unique org-specific prefix before any solution is created for production. Components created under the default publisher cannot be easily re-named or re-published.

Why it's wrong: Every user in the tenant has access to the Default environment. Without DLP, any connector can be used with any data. Without governance, it becomes a dumping ground for ad-hoc apps and flows that no one owns.

Fix: Apply a restrictive DLP policy to the Default environment immediately. Consider enabling Managed Environments for it. Publish a clear message: "Default environment = personal productivity only. Team and production apps go through the intake process."

Why it's wrong: Every deployment to production carries risk. Without a rollback plan, when something breaks you are in a high-pressure situation with no pre-agreed recovery path.

Fix: Before every production deployment: (1) Document the current production solution version. (2) Confirm the prior managed solution .zip is available to re-import. (3) Test the rollback procedure in UAT. (4) Include rollback steps in the change ticket.

🏗️ Architect Note — Sequencing

The most common mistake in this role is trying to do everything at once. Sequence matters: governance first (DLP + env strategy), then ALM next (solutions + pipeline), then enablement (CoE + citizen dev). You can't enable what you can't govern, and you can't govern what you haven't inventoried.
