Executive Summary

A Senior Business Analyst at TEKsystems has initiated a project to build a SharePoint Online Hub Site that will serve as a shared collaboration space for three internal teams — Sales, Solutions, and Delivery — within the Microsoft Practice. The request was routed via a peer recommendation, indicating organic grassroots demand rather than a top-down mandate.

The initiative is in its earliest stage: no requirements exist, no architecture has been approved, and the primary problem statement has not been formally articulated. This makes the upcoming discovery meeting a critical inflection point.

What Problem May This Be Solving?

Hypothesized ProblemLikelihoodEvidence
Content is scattered across email, Teams channels, and personal drivesHighCommon in practice-level teams without a content hub
Teams can't find each other's reusable assets (proposals, playbooks, templates)HighCross-functional nature of Sales + Solutions + Delivery
No single place for deal-related or delivery-related knowledgeMediumImplied by multi-team scope
Simpplr intranet doesn't serve team-level operational contentMediumSimpplr is org-wide; teams need something more contextual
Desire to improve internal visibility of solutions and delivery excellenceMediumHub sites are often used as a showcase / discovery layer

Why a Hub Site May Be Relevant

  • A SharePoint Hub Site provides a unifying navigation, search, and branding layer across multiple associated sites — ideal when content belongs to distinct teams but must be findable across them
  • Hub-level search rolls up across all associated sites, enabling cross-team findability without collapsing all content into one site
  • Hub navigation, news rollups, and highlighted content web parts allow each team to maintain editorial control while sharing a common landing experience
  • It is a modern SharePoint Online pattern — no subsites, no deeply nested hierarchies, no legacy feature debt
ℹ️
Why Discovery Comes FirstA hub site is infrastructure — it shapes how all downstream content is organized, found, and governed. Designing it before understanding user journeys, content types, permissions boundaries, and ownership models produces a site that looks good on day one and becomes a liability by month three. This meeting should generate requirements, not approvals.

Why This Could Go Wrong Without Discovery

  • SharePoint, Teams, and Simpplr all partially solve parts of this problem — without clarity, the hub risks duplicating two existing tools
  • Three teams sharing one hub means three sets of governance assumptions — and one missing content owner sinks the whole structure
  • Over-scoped hubs with no clear purpose become "link dumps" — built, launched, and abandoned within 6 months

Assumptions to Validate

These assumptions are currently being treated as likely true. Each must be explicitly confirmed or refuted during or after the meeting — building on a false assumption here has compounding consequences downstream.

🔒 Assumption 1 — Internal-Only Audience

Assumption: All users are Allegis Group / TEKsystems employees with corporate M365 accounts. No external guests, clients, or partners will access the hub.

Why it matters: External sharing requires different permissions configuration, potential licensing implications (AAD B2B), and sensitivity label enforcement. If even one team needs to share a document with a client, the entire permissions model changes.

Validate by asking: "Are there any scenarios where content would be shared with, or need to be visible to, people outside Allegis Group?" and "Do any of the three teams work with external collaborators on shared documents?"

🤝 Assumption 2 — Collaboration = Async Document & Knowledge Sharing

Assumption: "Collaboration" in this context means structured content sharing, document co-authoring, and knowledge discovery — not real-time chat, video calls, or task management.

Why it matters: If the real need is real-time co-authoring or team communication, Teams is a better fit. SharePoint is optimised for structured, persistent, findable content — not ephemeral conversation.

Validate by asking: "When you imagine two team members collaborating on a proposal, what does that actually look like? Are they working in the same document simultaneously, or reviewing and passing documents?"

🏗️ Assumption 3 — Topology: Hub + 3 Associated Functional Sites

Assumption: The hub site is a navigation and discovery layer; Sales, Solutions, and Delivery each get their own associated site with their own content, permissions, and ownership.

Why it matters: This is a reasonable starting hypothesis but may be wrong. Some teams may share content tightly enough to warrant one site. There may be additional cross-functional sites needed (e.g., deal pursuit, shared playbooks, delivery excellence). One large team may not want their content visible to others.

Validate by asking: "Should Sales be able to see everything in Solutions and Delivery by default? Or should some content be access-controlled?" and "Is there content that crosses all three teams — things like proposal templates or shared playbooks?"

🏢 Assumption 4 — Simpplr Serves a Different Purpose

Assumption: Simpplr is the top-level org-wide intranet (corporate comms, HR, policies) and the new hub is for team-level operational content. They serve different audiences and content types and do not overlap significantly.

Why it matters: If significant content will duplicate Simpplr, the hub creates confusion about where to go and fragments the digital workplace. Alternatively, if Simpplr is underused or poorly adopted, the hub may be an attempt to replace it — a bigger scope than what was stated.

Validate by asking: "Walk me through what lives in Simpplr today that the Sales, Solutions, or Delivery teams actually use. Is there content that will now live in both places?"

💬 Assumption 5 — Teams Already Exists but Doesn't Solve the Problem

Assumption: Microsoft Teams is actively used for chat and meetings. There are probably Teams channels for each team. However, Teams file storage is unstructured, findability is poor across teams, and there is no publishing layer — hence the interest in SharePoint.

Why it matters: Teams is already a valid partial solution. The hub should complement Teams (structured knowledge + discovery) rather than replace Teams (comms + collaboration). If this boundary isn't agreed, users will be confused about where to put content.

Validate by asking: "Do the teams already have Teams channels? If so, what kinds of content are being stored there now? What is the frustration with that experience?"

🎯 Assumption 6 — This Is a Mix of Communication and Knowledge Management

Assumption: The hub will serve two purposes simultaneously: (a) publishing / broadcasting news and updates and (b) structured knowledge storage and retrieval. These require different design patterns — communication sites for broadcasting, team sites for collaborative storage.

Why it matters: Designing only for one mode will frustrate users expecting the other. A hub site can be a communication site; associated sites may be team sites. Getting this mix right drives the entire content model and permissions approach.

Validate by asking: "Who will publish content to the hub homepage? A small editorial team, or can anyone post? And separately — who needs to store and retrieve working documents?"

Discovery Questions for the Meeting

These questions are organized by category. Use them to guide the meeting conversation. Not all questions need to be asked in the first meeting — prioritize based on what you hear in the room. The goal is to reveal the real collaboration problem, not just validate the proposed solution.

🎯 Business Goals & Success Criteria
What specific problem is this initiative trying to solve? Can you describe a concrete scenario where the current situation is failing?
Why it matters: Without a named problem, you're designing a solution for a hypothesis. A concrete scenario (e.g., "a sales rep can't find the latest proposal template and re-creates it from scratch") gives you a testable user story.
What does success look like at 30, 60, and 90 days after launch?
Why it matters: Forces clarity on whether this is about adoption metrics, content published, problems resolved, or time saved.
Who is the business owner for this initiative and who will champion adoption?
Why it matters: Without an executive sponsor or named champion, adoption will be passive and governance will be ignored.
Is there a deadline or event driving this (e.g., a new team onboarding, a reorganization, a product launch)?
Why it matters: Artificial deadlines drive scope decisions. Understanding the driver helps you separate must-haves from nice-to-haves.
If this hub didn't exist 6 months from now, what would the impact be?
Why it matters: The inverse test. If the answer is "not much," the initiative may be a nice-to-have, not a must-have — and that changes how you scope it.
👥 Audience & Personas
Who are the primary users of this hub — are they mostly consumers of content or active contributors?
Why it matters: Drives the permissions model, content publishing workflow, and information architecture design decisions.
Within Sales, Solutions, and Delivery — are there sub-teams, practices, or geographic splits that need different access?
Why it matters: What appears to be 3 sites may actually be 6 or more once sub-teams are mapped.
What is the approximate total user count and what's the split between readers and editors?
Why it matters: Informs licensing review (is Viva a factor?), and helps scope the governance model.
Are there users who are not full-time employees (contractors, partners)? Do they need access?
Why it matters: External access changes the entire permission and compliance posture.
📄 Content Types & Ownership
What types of content will live here — documents, pages, lists, news posts, links, embedded reports?
Why it matters: Content type determines whether you need a library, a list, a pages library, or an embedded Power BI report — they are designed very differently.
Who owns each category of content? Who creates it, reviews it, approves it, and removes it when it's stale?
Why it matters: Governance without named stewards means content rots. Every document library or page section needs an owner.
Is there content that needs to be kept under version control or requires formal approval before publishing?
Why it matters: Major / minor versioning and approval workflows in SharePoint are powerful but add process overhead — use them deliberately.
How long does content remain valid? Should old content be archived, deleted, or just left?
Why it matters: Content lifecycle governance must be defined before launch or the site accumulates outdated material within months.
🏛️ Information & Site Architecture
Is the proposed topology (hub + Sales site + Solutions site + Delivery site) correct, or are there additional functional areas to consider?
Why it matters: Cross-functional sites (deal pursuit, shared templates, delivery excellence) may be needed and are commonly missed in early design sessions.
Should teams be able to see each other's content by default, or should some areas be access-controlled?
Why it matters: Security-trimmed navigation means users only see what they have access to. This is the most common permissions mistake in hub site design.
What should live on the hub homepage vs on the associated sites?
Why it matters: The hub home is a discovery and navigation layer — it should surface content, not store it. Conflating hub home with team storage is a common design error.
What are the top 3–5 tasks a user should be able to complete within 60 seconds of landing on the hub?
Why it matters: Forces the design conversation into user journeys rather than feature lists.
🔍 Search, Metadata & Taxonomy
How do users currently find content — keyword search, browsing folders, asking colleagues?
Why it matters: SharePoint search is powerful but requires metadata and good content titles to work. If users rely on folder browsing, the taxonomy design needs to match that mental model.
Are there common attributes that would be useful to filter or search on (e.g., team, content type, year, practice area, status)?
Why it matters: These become managed metadata columns — they are the backbone of structured content findability.
Is there an existing taxonomy or term store in the M365 tenant that should be reused?
Why it matters: Duplicate term stores create inconsistency. If a taxonomy exists, extend it; don't create a parallel one.
🔐 Permissions & Security
Should all three teams have read access to each other's sites by default, or is some content restricted?
Why it matters: Hub sites share navigation and search, but permissions are per-site. Open-by-default vs need-to-know is a fundamental governance decision.
Are there any sensitivity labels or DLP policies currently in place in the tenant that would apply to content here?
Why it matters: If the tenant has M365 Purview policies, they may apply automatically to SharePoint libraries — this affects what can be stored and who can share it.
Who will be Site Owners on each site? What is the escalation path if a site owner leaves?
Why it matters: Orphaned sites are one of the most common governance failures — a site with no active owner drifts out of compliance and eventually gets abandoned.
🤝 Microsoft Teams Integration
Which of the three teams already has a Teams team or channel? What content lives there that would overlap with the hub?
Why it matters: Avoids building what Teams already provides. Also helps position SharePoint as the structured, searchable layer vs Teams as the ephemeral collaboration space.
Would users want to access SharePoint content directly from within Teams (as a tab)?
Why it matters: SharePoint pages and document libraries can be pinned as tabs in Teams — this can dramatically increase adoption if users don't want to leave Teams to find documents.
Should the hub or associated sites be Group-connected (with a Teams backing team)?
Why it matters: Group-connected sites come with a Teams team, Planner, OneNote, etc. For a hub / communication site, this is usually not desired. For associated functional sites, it may be.
📏 Governance & Lifecycle
Is there an existing SharePoint governance policy in the tenant (naming conventions, provisioning process, site templates)?
Why it matters: You may not be able to create sites ad-hoc; there may be an approval or provisioning workflow already in place.
Who is responsible for the long-term health of the hub — content reviews, user feedback, analytics?
Why it matters: A launch without a named ongoing steward is a site with a 6-month lifespan. The governance model must be agreed before go-live.
Will there be any compliance, retention, or records management requirements on any of the content?
Why it matters: If yes, retention labels or records management policies must be designed before content is uploaded — not after.
🎨 Branding, UX & Adoption
Is there a standard M365 / SharePoint branding guideline already in use in the tenant?
Why it matters: Applying a theme and logo that's already approved reduces IT/comms review overhead and ensures brand consistency.
What is the adoption and training plan? Who will onboard users to the hub?
Why it matters: No adoption plan = no adoption. The best-designed hub fails if no one knows it exists or how to use it.
Will usage analytics be monitored? Is there an expectation to report on page views, active users, or search queries?
Why it matters: SharePoint has built-in site usage analytics — but someone must own reviewing them and acting on the data.
📦 Migration & Content Sourcing
What is the source of content that will populate the hub at launch? Is there a migration effort involved?
Why it matters: A hub site launch with zero content is a failed launch. Content migration scope dramatically affects timelines and may require SPMT or ShareGate tooling.
Is there any content in Teams file tabs, OneDrive personal drives, or shared network drives that should move to SharePoint?
Why it matters: This is where most organizational content actually lives — on network shares and personal drives. Identifying it early allows for realistic scope planning.

Brainstorming Topics & Design Discussion Points

These are the strategic questions worth debating in the room — not questions to ask the stakeholder, but design decisions to work through collaboratively. Bring these as prompts, not as a prepared answer.

When Should Collaboration Happen in Teams vs SharePoint?

The clearest mental model: Teams for conversations, SharePoint for content. Teams channels are great for real-time discussion, quick file sharing, and project-level chat. SharePoint is better for persistent, findable, structured content that outlives any single project or conversation. The practical test: "Would someone need to find this 6 months from now without knowing who created it?" If yes, it belongs in SharePoint.

Publishing a how-to guideLink to SP page✅ Primary
Meeting notes and follow-ups✅ OneNote / TeamsArchive only if needed
Reusable templates library❌ Hard to find✅ Primary
News / announcements for a teamChannel post (ephemeral)✅ News post (persistent)

Should the Hub Be a Navigation / Discovery Layer — Not a Storage Layer?

A common design mistake is treating the hub homepage as a place to store content. It should be a place to surface content. The hub homepage should aggregate: news from associated sites, highlighted documents, quick links, and cross-team search. The actual documents and pages should live in the associated sites. If users are uploading files directly to the hub, the architecture is wrong.

Should Each Function Have Its Own Associated Site?

The 1-hub + 3-associated-sites model is a solid starting hypothesis. Reasons it makes sense:

  • Sales, Solutions, and Delivery have distinct audiences, content types, and ownership structures
  • Permissions can be managed independently (a delivery playbook may not be visible to all Sales staff)
  • Each team can have their own news feed, page library, and document structure

Reasons to reconsider:

  • If one team is much smaller or less active, their site may become a ghost town
  • If significant content is shared across all three (e.g., proposal templates, pricing guidance), a shared content site may be needed as a fourth site

Are There Cross-Functional Sites Missing from the Proposal?

💡
Consider a 4th or 5th SiteCross-functional content — deal pursuits, shared solution accelerators, delivery excellence case studies — often doesn't belong to one team. Without a dedicated home, it ends up duplicated across all three sites or lost. A "Practice Hub" or "Shared Resources" associated site may be the most important site in the topology.

What Is the Minimum Viable Hub?

If you had to ship something useful in 4 weeks, what would it be? A strong MVP might be:

  1. Hub communication site with branding, navigation, and a news rollup web part
  2. One associated site (whichever team has the most content ready) with a document library and 2–3 pages
  3. A shared quick links section on the hub homepage
  4. One named site owner per site, a governance doc, and a content review schedule

Everything else — audience targeting, Viva Connections, Power Automate approvals, custom metadata — is phase 2.

Adoption Risk: Does This Duplicate Teams or Simpplr?

⚠️
The "Where Do I Put It?" ProblemIf users have three places that all accept documents (Teams files, SharePoint, Simpplr), adoption will split and content will be fragmented. The single most important adoption decision is a clear, communicated, enforced rule about what type of content lives where. Without this, all three tools become equally authoritative and equally unreliable.

Could Viva Connections Be Relevant?

If the org uses Microsoft Viva, the hub site can be set as the home site and surfaced through Viva Connections in Teams. This means users get a SharePoint-quality content experience without leaving Teams — which significantly increases adoption. This is worth exploring if the org has Viva licensing and if Teams is the primary communication channel.

SharePoint Online Solution Areas to Cover

A comprehensive checklist of SPO capabilities, patterns, and governance topics that are relevant to this initiative. Use this to ensure nothing architectural is missed during design conversations.

Site Architecture

  • 🔷Hub Sites vs Associated Sites: Hub provides unified navigation, search scope, and branding. Associated sites retain independent permissions and ownership. A site can only be associated with one hub.
  • 🔷Communication Sites vs Team Sites: Comm sites are publishing-first (one-to-many, no M365 group). Team sites are collaboration-first (group-connected, with Teams, Planner, OneNote). Hub should be a comm site; associated sites may be either depending on use case.
  • 🔷M365 Group-Connected Sites: Team sites created via Teams or SharePoint SharePoint admin are group-connected. This creates a backing M365 group with shared inbox, calendar, Planner. Useful for team working spaces; not needed for publishing sites.
  • 🔷No Subsites: Modern SharePoint governance strongly discourages subsites. Each logical area should be a separate site collection associated to the hub — not a subsite of the hub.
  • 🔷Site Provisioning: Check whether the tenant has a governed provisioning process (e.g., SharePoint admin approval, PnP Provisioning, site request form). Don't create sites ad-hoc if a process exists.

Navigation Design

  • 🔷Hub Navigation: Appears on all associated sites. Max ~6 items. Should link to hub home + key cross-team resources. Must be kept simple.
  • 🔷Local Navigation: Each associated site has its own left-hand navigation. Owners control this independently.
  • 🔷Footer / Global Links: Consider a consistent footer on hub and associated sites for support, governance contacts, and feedback links.
  • 🔷Audience Targeting in Navigation: Hub nav links can be targeted to security groups. Use this if some navigation items are only relevant to one team.

Content & Libraries

  • 🔷Document Libraries: Primary storage for files. Enable versioning (major/minor). Consider check-out requirement for controlled documents. Flat folder structure with metadata is better than deeply nested folders.
  • 🔷Content Types: Define reusable content types (e.g., "Proposal Template", "Playbook", "Case Study") with standard metadata columns. Apply at the site collection or hub level for consistency.
  • 🔷Metadata / Managed Taxonomy: Use SharePoint term store for shared taxonomy (e.g., Practice Area, Content Status, Team). Enables cross-site filtering and consistent search refiners.
  • 🔷Lists: Use SharePoint lists for structured data (contacts, FAQs, project registry, links directory). Not for document storage.
  • 🔷News: News posts roll up via hub. Each associated site can publish news independently. The hub homepage aggregates all news across the hub with the News web part.
  • 🔷Highlighted Content / Rollups: Highlighted Content Web Part (HCWP) is the primary tool for dynamic content rollup across sites. Can filter by content type, metadata, date, and more.

Search & Findability

  • 🔷Hub-Scoped Search: Search from any hub-associated site searches the entire hub by default. Managed properties and refiner configuration may be needed for optimal results.
  • 🔷Security Trimming: Search results are always security-trimmed. Users will only see content they have access to — even in hub-scoped search.
  • 🔷Metadata Columns as Refiners: Well-designed managed metadata columns become search refiners. This is the primary reason to invest in a taxonomy early.
  • 🔷People Search: SharePoint search includes people profiles from Azure AD. Useful for "find an expert" scenarios.

Permissions & Security

  • 🔷Hub permissions ≠ Associated site permissions: Associating a site to a hub does NOT share permissions. Each site manages its own permission groups (Owners / Members / Visitors).
  • 🔷Security Groups over individual permissions: Always use AAD security groups or M365 groups for site permissions. Never add individuals directly — it becomes unmanageable at scale.
  • 🔷Sharing Settings: Configure sharing settings at the site level (who can share, with whom, link expiry). Hub sharing settings don't cascade to associated sites.
  • 🔷External Sharing: If confirmed internal-only, disable external sharing at the tenant or site level for all hub-associated sites.

Governance, Lifecycle & Compliance

  • 🔷Site Lifecycle Policy: Implement an inactive site review process. M365 admin center can identify stale sites. Define what happens to a site when a team is restructured.
  • 🔷Retention Labels: If content has retention requirements (e.g., proposals kept 7 years), apply M365 Purview retention labels to document libraries.
  • 🔷Sensitivity Labels: If DLP policies apply, ensure sensitivity labels are configured for SharePoint libraries containing sensitive content.
  • 🔷Usage Analytics: SharePoint has built-in site usage reports (page views, unique visitors, popular content). Assign someone to review these monthly and act on low-engagement signals.
  • 🔷Content Review Schedule: Every associated site should have a documented content review schedule (monthly for news, quarterly for libraries, annually for archived content).

Branding, UX & Accessibility

  • 🔷SharePoint Themes: Apply a consistent theme (colour palette) at the hub level. Associated sites can inherit or customise within the brand.
  • 🔷Site Logo & Header: Each site should have a consistent logo and header style. The hub logo anchors the brand.
  • 🔷Audience Targeting (Web Parts): SharePoint supports audience targeting on individual web parts — show different content to Sales vs Delivery without separate pages.
  • 🔷Accessibility: SharePoint OOB web parts are accessible (WCAG 2.1 AA). Avoid custom code or embedded iframes that may not meet accessibility standards.
  • 🔷Mobile (SharePoint App): SharePoint mobile app supports hub navigation. Consider mobile experience if teams are on the go.

Integration Opportunities

  • 🔷Power Automate: Document approval workflows, content review reminders, new-file notification flows, and list item routing are all strong SharePoint + Power Automate patterns.
  • 🔷Power Apps: Embed custom forms or lightweight apps as SharePoint web parts for scenarios where OOB list forms are insufficient.
  • 🔷Microsoft Forms: Embed feedback forms, intake forms, or surveys directly on SharePoint pages.
  • 🔷Power BI: Embed team dashboards directly in SharePoint pages using the Power BI web part — no separate BI portal needed.
  • 🔷Microsoft Stream: Training videos, recorded demos, and onboarding content can be stored in Stream and surfaced via the Stream web part on SharePoint pages.
  • 🔷Viva Connections: If the org licenses Viva, the hub can become the home site surfaced through Teams — bridging the Teams vs SharePoint access divide.

Content Lifecycle Rules (Define Before Launch)

  • Active content: Reviewed quarterly by content owner. Updated in place with versioning.

Architect Guidance — What the Hub Should & Should Not Do

This section provides opinionated architectural direction for stakeholders and delivery teams. It clarifies the true purpose of a SharePoint Hub Site, defines clear boundaries, and establishes design principles to guide every subsequent decision. Use this to anchor the conversation whenever scope drift or misconceptions surface.

A. What a Hub Site SHOULD Provide

  • ✅A shared navigation experience across related sites. Hub navigation appears consistently on every associated site, giving users a coherent cross-team wayfinding experience without duplicating navigation on each site.
  • ✅Common branding and theme consistency. The hub enforces a visual identity (logo, colour palette, header style) across all associated sites — each site feels like part of the same space without being identical.
  • ✅A central landing page for discovery, orientation, and wayfinding. The hub homepage helps users understand what exists, where to go, and what is happening across the practice — it is an entry point, not a filing cabinet.
  • ✅Roll-up of news, events, highlighted content, and hub-scoped search. News posts published on associated sites surface on the hub homepage. Search from the hub queries all associated sites, security-trimmed by default.
  • ✅A logical grouping of related sites by function or business area. The hub binds Sales, Solutions, Delivery (and Shared Resources) into a coherent family — without merging their content, permissions, or ownership.
  • ✅A scalable modern information architecture based on separate site collections. Each function gets its own site collection with its own governance, owners, and content lifecycle. The hub is the connective tissue, not the container.
  • ✅A publishing and discoverability layer for cross-functional visibility. Content lives where it is created (associated sites); the hub surfaces it where it needs to be found (hub homepage, hub search, highlighted content rollups).
  • ✅Curated resources, links, tools, and entry points. Quick links, promoted results, and curated web parts on the hub homepage guide users to the most important destinations without requiring them to know where things live.
  • ✅A way to connect related team and communication sites while preserving their independence. Each associated site retains its own permission model, navigation, and ownership. Association to the hub adds discoverability, not dependency.
  • ✅A solution that adapts to business change without URL or hierarchy rigidity. Sites can be re-associated, renamed, or replaced without breaking the hub structure. Modern SharePoint avoids the deep URL path trap of classic subsite architectures.

B. What a Hub Site SHOULD NOT Become

  • ✗A replacement for Microsoft Teams chat and conversation-based collaboration. SharePoint has no messaging layer. Attempting to use page comments, list discussions, or news replies as a Teams substitute creates a poor user experience and fragments communication.
  • ✗A document dumping ground with no metadata, structure, or ownership. Uploading files to the hub without content types, metadata columns, or named owners produces a search-hostile, ungovernable repository within weeks.
  • ✗A single monolithic site for all three teams. One site means shared permissions, a single owner, a navigation structure that serves no team perfectly, and content conflicts at scale. Three teams need three sites, connected by a hub.
  • ✗A permissions inheritance mechanism. This is the most common misunderstanding. Associating a site to a hub does not grant users access to that site. Permissions are site-by-site. Hub association adds navigation and search scope only.
  • ✗A rigid org-chart clone if the collaboration model is process- or activity-based. If teams collaborate cross-functionally on deal pursuits or delivery excellence (not by function), the topology should reflect work patterns, not reporting lines.
  • ✗A substitute for governance, content lifecycle, and stewardship. Technology cannot compensate for absent ownership. A hub with no governance model produces stale, unreliable content that erodes user trust and reduces adoption to zero.
  • ✗A heavily customised portal requiring developer intervention to maintain. SPFx extensions, custom web parts, and bespoke layouts that require a developer to update a page create a maintenance dependency that kills the long-term health of the site.
  • ✗A duplicate of Simpplr or another intranet platform without a clearly differentiated purpose. If users cannot articulate why they would go to SharePoint rather than Simpplr, they will not go to SharePoint. The boundary must be explicit and communicated.
  • ✗A classic subsite-heavy architecture disguised as modern SharePoint. Subsites were deprecated for good reason. Creating nested subsites under the hub — even with a modern skin — reintroduces all the navigation, permission, and URL fragility problems of classic SharePoint.
  • ✗A mixed space where temporary working-team content and long-term published knowledge coexist without clear boundaries. Deal working files, in-progress drafts, and completed playbooks should have distinct homes. Mixing them makes search results unreliable and content lifecycle management impossible.

C. Architect Recommendation — Positioning Statement

🏛️
Architect Position "The hub should be positioned primarily as a discovery, navigation, publishing, and knowledge access layer that connects related collaboration and communication spaces. Day-to-day active collaboration may still happen in Teams and associated team sites, while the hub provides cross-functional visibility, findability, consistency, and curated access to shared assets. It is the front door to the practice's knowledge — not the room where the work happens."

In one sentence: Build the hub for people who need to find things. Build Teams channels and team sites for people who need to do things.

D. Design Principles

🏗️Flat architecture over deep hierarchy
Site collections + hub association beat subsite trees. Every nesting level adds navigation debt, permission complexity, and URL fragility.
🔲Separate sites for separate units of work
Sales, Solutions, Delivery, and Shared Resources are distinct enough to warrant distinct sites. One site for all is an anti-pattern.
📋Working content vs curated/published content
In-progress work files belong in team sites or Teams. Finished, reusable, authoritative content belongs in governed libraries and pages.
🏷️Metadata and findability over folder sprawl
Invest in 4–6 mandatory metadata columns before any content is uploaded. Folders are a crutch; metadata is infrastructure.
🛡️Governance and ownership by design — not retrofit
Name a site owner and content steward for every site before provisioning. Governance added post-launch is never enforced retroactively.
🔐Least-privilege and intentional permissions
Default to read-only access at the hub level. Elevate permissions deliberately and via security groups — never via individual direct grants.
📦OOB capabilities before customisation
Modern SharePoint's out-of-the-box web parts cover 90% of use cases. Every line of custom code is a future maintenance debt.
🚀MVP first, then iterate based on evidence
Launch a small, complete, governable hub rather than a large, incomplete, ungoverned one. Add sites and features based on measured adoption signals.
🗺️Navigation aligned to user tasks, not org chart
Design hub and site navigation around what users need to do, not how the org is structured. An account executive and a delivery manager have different journeys.
🚫Avoid duplication with Teams and Simpplr
Every content type must have one authoritative home. If it can live in any of three tools, it will live in all three — and be reliably found in none.
🔧Design for maintainability and future scaling
Every design decision should be answerable: "Can the site owner update this without a developer?" If not, reconsider.
👤Prioritise adoption and clarity over feature richness
A hub with three useful things that everyone uses beats one with thirty features that no one understands. Simplicity is a feature.

These are the failure modes that kill SharePoint hub initiatives. Raise them in the meeting — not to be pessimistic, but to get early agreement on how to prevent them.

🚨
The #1 Risk: No Named Owner Post-LaunchMore SharePoint sites fail because of governance gaps than technical failures. If no one is accountable for keeping content fresh, the site becomes unreliable within 3 months and abandoned within 6. Every site needs a named owner before go-live — not after.

Governance Gaps That Kill Hubs Silently

  • Orphaned sites: Site owner leaves → no one knows who owns it → permissions rot, content stagnates
  • Permission creep: Direct permission grants accumulate over time; regular access reviews never happen
  • Stale navigation: Hub nav links to sites that were renamed, moved, or deleted
  • Sensitivity label drift: A new sensitivity policy applies to the tenant but old content wasn't retroactively labeled
  • Teams + SharePoint divergence: A Teams channel stores the "real" version of files; SharePoint becomes a shadow copy
⚠️
The Simpplr / Teams Overlap RiskIf this hub successfully solves the content findability problem but Simpplr is still the place for org news and Teams is still where files actually live day-to-day, the hub becomes a third destination competing for attention. The architecture is only sustainable if there's a clear, communicated, and enforced boundary between all three.

Proposed Next Steps

A phased plan from discovery through adoption. Not all phases need to be sequential — some can overlap. The key is that each phase has a named owner and a concrete output before the next begins.

  1. Discovery Workshop (Week 1–2) — Run structured stakeholder interviews with Sales, Solutions, and Delivery representatives. Use the question bank from Section 6. Output: validated requirements doc, confirmed use cases, agreed site topology. Owner: BA (A) + Architect (B).
  2. Stakeholder Mapping (Week 2) — Identify site owners, content stewards, and approvers for each site. Document in a RACI. Confirm executive sponsor. Without this, governance collapses. Owner: BA.
  3. Information Architecture Exercise (Week 2–3) — Card sort or tree test with 5–8 representative users. Validate navigation structure and content categories. Agree on metadata taxonomy. Owner: Architect.
  4. Content Inventory (Week 3) — Each team audits their existing content (Teams files, email attachments, network shares). Categorize: keep, migrate, create new, discard. Identify content gaps that would make the site empty at launch. Owner: Team content stewards.
  5. Governance Definition (Week 3–4) — Document: permission model, naming conventions, content lifecycle rules, publishing workflow, review schedule, ownership succession plan. Must be agreed before provisioning begins. Owner: Architect + ISM (F).
  6. Prototype / Wireframe (Week 4) — Build a low-fidelity wireframe of the hub homepage and one associated site in SharePoint (use a test site collection). Walk stakeholders through the layout. Gather feedback before building for real. Owner: Architect.
  7. MVP Build (Week 5–6) — Provision hub site and first 1–2 associated sites per agreed topology. Apply branding, navigation, and web part layout. Seed with real content from inventory. Owner: Architect + site owners.
  8. Pilot Rollout (Week 7) — Release to a small group (5–10 users per team). Gather structured feedback via a Microsoft Form. Monitor site analytics. Fix blockers before full launch. Owner: BA + site owners.
  9. Training & Adoption (Week 8) — Short demo recording, a "how to use this site" page on the hub, a Teams announcement, and a named champion per team. Schedule a 30-day and 90-day review. Owner: BA + champions.

Parallel Track: Governance Before Build

🚨
Do Not Skip GovernanceThe temptation is to provision the sites first and "figure out governance later." This always results in permission creep, stale content, and confused users. Governance must be defined — even as a one-page document — before the first site is created. It takes 2 hours to write; it saves months of cleanup.

What a 90-Day Success Review Should Cover

  • Monthly active users per site (SharePoint analytics)
  • Most-viewed pages and documents — does the top content match the intended use cases?
  • Search terms that returned no results — what are users looking for that isn't there?
  • Content published vs content planned — is the governance model being followed?
  • User satisfaction via a simple pulse survey
  • Owners review: are all named owners still in their roles?

Suggested Meeting Agenda

A structured 55-minute agenda for the discovery meeting. Adjust timing based on attendee count and conversational depth — the goal is to leave the last 10 minutes for agreed actions, not still discussing scope.

0–5 min
Introductions & Meeting Purpose
Brief round of introductions. Set the framing: "This meeting is about understanding the problem before we design the solution." Confirm that no architecture decision will be made today — this is discovery.
5–15 min
Problem Statement — What Are We Solving?
Ask: "Walk me through a concrete scenario where the current situation is failing." Let the BA and any team reps describe the pain points. Listen for: where content currently lives, how users find things today, what the frustration is. Do not jump to solutions yet.
15–25 min
Stakeholder & Audience Discussion
Who are the users? How many? What do they need to do? Are there personas — readers vs publishers vs admins? Any users outside Allegis Group? Confirm internal-only. Surface any access or permissions complexity early.
25–35 min
Platform Boundary Discussion — SharePoint vs Teams vs Simpplr
Present the three-tool boundary model (Section 9). Ask: "What does each team already use today? What lives in Teams that should live in SharePoint? What do you expect Simpplr to keep doing?" Get agreement on where the boundaries are before designing the hub.
35–45 min
First-Pass Architecture Review
Walk through the proposed topology (Section 10): hub + Sales + Solutions + Delivery + optional Shared Resources. Ask: "Does this map to how you think about the teams? Is there content that doesn't fit any of these buckets? Should Sales see Delivery's content by default?" Capture changes to the topology.
45–50 min
Governance & Ownership
Ask: "Who will own each site? Who can publish pages? What happens when content gets stale?" Confirm that named owners exist for each proposed site before the meeting ends — this is a launch blocker.
50–55 min
Agreed Actions & Next Steps
Capture: decisions made, decisions deferred, open questions, and named owners for each action. Schedule the next touchpoint (working session or prototype review). Do not leave without a named action owner for each next step.

Key Takeaway Summary

The five things that matter most for this initiative to succeed:

🎯
1. Validate the Problem Before Validating the SolutionA SharePoint Hub Site was proposed as the answer before the question was fully formed. The first job of this meeting is to confirm: What is the real pain? Is a hub site the right answer? Could Teams or Simpplr serve this need with less effort? Don't build what was requested — build what is needed.
✅
2. Governance Is Not Optional — It's the Launch BlockerThe most common cause of SharePoint hub failure is not technical — it's governance. Named site owners, a content lifecycle policy, and a publishing model must be agreed before the first site is provisioned. Without these, the hub will be well-designed, poorly maintained, and abandoned.
🔗
3. Draw the Line Between SharePoint, Teams, and Simpplr — Then Enforce ItThe hub only works if users know what lives where. This boundary must be explicit, communicated, and reinforced through training and design choices. If content can live in any of the three tools, it will live in all three — and be reliably found in none.
⚠️
4. Seed the Site Before Launch — Empty Hubs Don't Get AdoptedA hub site with great structure but no content fails at launch. Plan for content seeding as part of the build phase. Each site needs at least 10–20 real, useful documents and 2–3 published pages on day one. The migration and content creation effort must be resourced — it won't happen on its own.
🚀
5. Ship Small, Measure Early, IterateThe strongest possible MVP is a hub site with one or two associated sites, real content, clear governance, and a named owner — launched to a pilot group within 6 weeks. Add sites, features, and automation based on what users actually need, not what was theorized in the design session. Measure at 30, 60, and 90 days. Kill what isn't being used.

Define the problem before the solution
Name a site owner before you provision a site
Metadata first, folders never
Hub = navigation and discovery, not storage
SharePoint, Teams, and Simpplr are not competitors — they need boundaries, not a winner
